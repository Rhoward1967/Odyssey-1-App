PART I: THE MONETARY INSTRUMENT QUESTION

1.1 The Origin of the Inquiry

The foundational question arose from a real-world circumstance: a court ordering an individual on disability — effectively insolvent — to pay a financial judgment in thousands of dollars. This raised an immediate philosophical question: what exactly is the court demanding payment in, and what is the legal nature of that instrument?



1.2 The Constitutional Argument

Article I, Section 8 of the United States Constitution explicitly grants Congress the power to coin money and regulate its value. The Federal Reserve Act of 1913 effectively delegated this authority to a private central banking system — the Federal Reserve — operating outside normal constitutional checks and balances.



This delegation has been acknowledged, directly or indirectly, by prominent figures including:

Marriner Eccles, Federal Reserve Chairman — on-record admissions regarding money creation through debt

Milton Friedman — acknowledged structural concerns with the Federal Reserve system

Congressman Wright Patman — spent decades arguing the delegation was unconstitutional



1.3 The Practical Legal Reality

Despite the intellectual legitimacy of this argument, courts operate on a positivist legal framework — meaning they apply the law as it currently stands and is enforced, not as philosophical or constitutional theory might suggest it should be. Federal Reserve notes are valid legal tender under federal law. One hundred-plus years of ratified practice and stare decisis have cemented this position regardless of the merits of the underlying constitutional argument.



The critical observation however is this: by demanding payment in these instruments, and by being funded through and dependent upon the same monetary system, the court is not a neutral party in monetary disputes. It is a beneficiary.





PART II: THE CONFLICT OF INTEREST ANALYSIS

2.1 The Logical Chain

Justice requires impartiality. Impartiality requires the absence of conflict of interest. Courts have a material financial stake in the monetary system. Therefore, courts cannot be genuinely impartial in cases involving that system. Therefore, true justice is structurally compromised in those cases.

This is not radical reasoning — it is basic conflict of interest doctrine applied consistently to the institution that normally applies it to others.



2.2 The Irony of Judicial Disqualification

Courts will disqualify individual judges for comparatively minor conflicts of interest — a judge owning stock in a company being litigated, for example. Yet the systemic conflict described above — where the court as an institution is a direct beneficiary of the monetary system it is asked to adjudicate — is never subjected to the same scrutiny.



The standard applied to individuals within the system is orders of magnitude stricter than the standard applied to the institution itself.

PART III: THE PROBLEM OF PRECEDENT

3.1 What Stare Decisis Claims to Be

Stare decisis — the doctrine of precedent — claims to provide neutral, consistent, and predictable application of previously established legal reasoning. It is presented as a stabilizing force that prevents arbitrary judicial decision-making and ensures equal treatment under law.



3.2 The Compounding Bias Problem

If the original opinion was rendered by a court that had a conflict of interest, then every subsequent court that follows that precedent is:

Compounding the original bias

Institutionalizing the original conflict

Lending it increasing legitimacy purely through repetition

Never revisiting the foundational flaw in reasoning or circumstance



Precedent essentially argues: this is just because it was decided before. But that reasoning does not address whether the original decision was just, whether the original court was impartial, or whether the circumstances that produced the original decision remain valid. It assumes legitimacy through age and repetition alone.

3.5 The Self-Referencing Loop

Precedent creates a self-reinforcing cycle: a biased opinion is rendered, followed as precedent, cited repeatedly, becomes settled law, grows increasingly difficult to challenge, and the original bias is never examined. The case before the court becomes secondary to the historical opinion. The living human being with specific circumstances is reduced to fitting a pre-existing legal box — often one built under conditions that no longer exist.

4.2 The Institutional Exceptionalism Problem

What emerges from careful analysis is a pattern of institutional exceptionalism — the system that creates and enforces law places itself outside the reach of that same law, while simultaneously claiming to represent and uphold it. Every accountability mechanism within the legal system feeds back into the same institution: judicial conduct boards are staffed by judges, bar associations are governed by lawyers, and appeals are heard within the same system that produced the original decision.



In logic, this is called special pleading — claiming an exception to a rule for oneself without justifiable reason, while enforcing that rule on others. It is considered a logical fallacy and an ethical violation in virtually every philosophical framework.

Aquinas: An unjust law is no law at all — a system that exempts itself from justice produces unjust law by definition

Blackstone's Ratio: Better that ten guilty persons escape than one innocent suffer — reflecting that justice, not institutional outcomes, is the priority





PART V: CORE CONCLUSIONS (VERSION 1.0)



5.1 The System Condemns Itself by Its Own Rules

The most intellectually powerful aspect of this line of inquiry is that it requires no external standard. By taking the courts' own stated principles — impartiality, good faith, clean hands, nemo iudex in causa sua, rule of law — and applying them consistently and without exception to the institution itself, the system fails its own stated standard. No outside ideology or framework is required to reach this conclusion.



5.2 Imperfect Justice vs. No Justice

It must be acknowledged that imperfect justice is not the same as no justice. Courts do sometimes rule against powerful interests. The alternative to imperfect courts has historically produced worse outcomes for vulnerable people. These concessions do not resolve the structural contradiction — but they are important to hold in tension with the critique.



5.3 The Practical Reality for Vulnerable Individuals

For individuals navigating the system — particularly those who are economically vulnerable — the philosophical critique, however valid, provides no immediate relief. Practically speaking, Social Security Disability income carries federal exemption protections from garnishment. Bankruptcy proceedings can discharge certain civil judgments. Motions to modify can sometimes revisit payment terms based on demonstrated inability to pay. These practical remedies exist within the flawed system and may offer more immediate protection than challenging the system's foundations.



5.4 The Central Finding

A system cannot be truly impartial when it holds a material stake in the outcome. The courts are simultaneously referee and player in monetary disputes. This is not a conspiracy theory — it is an institutional design flaw of the highest order, one that legal scholars, philosophers, and even sitting justices have acknowledged in different contexts, without ever arriving at a structural resolution.



The gap between what courts claim and what they practice at the systemic level is not a minor inconsistency. It is a foundational contradiction — one this research intends to continue examining.
