PART I: THE CONSTITUTIONAL AUTHORITY CHAIN

1.1 Constitutional Basis

ESTABLISHED:

Article I Section 8 grants Congress the power to coin money and regulate its value. Neither provision was amended to authorize the Federal Reserve Act of 1913 or the delegation of monetary authority to a private banking system. That constitutional question has never been definitively resolved by the Supreme Court. Source: Constitutional text confirmed. Companion research Part IV confirmed.

1.2 National Banking Acts 1863 and 1864

ESTABLISHED:

The National Bank Act of 1863 was passed by a 23 to 21 Senate vote. It tied banking directly to government debt from the foundation — banks could issue notes only proportional to government bonds held. It established the OCC. National banks created were entirely privately owned. Source: Federal Reserve History confirmed.

1.3 Federal Reserve Act 1913

ESTABLISHED:

Passed December 23 1913 when approximately 30 senators had left. HR 2990 112th Congress stated: In 1913 Congress effectively delegated the sovereign power to create money to the Federal Reserve system and private financial industry. Source: Senate record confirmed. HR 2990 confirmed.

1.4 The 1930s Depression Response

ESTABLISHED:

The Federal Reserve's own historical record documents its failure during the Depression. The Banking Act of 1935 centralized monetary control in a seven-member Board of Governors — moving authority further from regional banks. Source: Federal Reserve History confirmed.

1.5 Nixon Decision 1971

ESTABLISHED:

Nixon closed the gold window August 15 1971 without congressional authorization — deliberately timed to avoid Congress per his own archived tapes. Federal debt grew from $398 billion to over $36 trillion. Middle-income households declined from 61% to 51% of Americans. Source: Federal Reserve History confirmed. Nixon tapes confirmed. Fraser Institute confirmed. Pew Research confirmed.

PART II: HOW MONEY IS CREATED IN A DEBT-BASED SYSTEM

2.1 The Bank of England Primary Source

ESTABLISHED:

The majority of money in the modern economy is created by commercial banks making loans. Banks do not act simply as intermediaries lending out deposits that savers place with them. Source: Bank of England Quarterly Bulletin 2014 Q1 — McLeay, Radia, and Thomas — confirmed primary source.

2.2 The Mechanism

ESTABLISHED:

Whenever a bank makes a loan it creates a deposit in the borrower's bank account thereby creating new money. 92% of deposits in the banking system resulted from lending activity — only 8% from cash deposits. Source: Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia confirmed.

2.3 Congressional Research Service

ESTABLISHED:

John B. Henderson, Senior Specialist in Price Economics, Congressional Research Service: Money is created when loans are issued and debts incurred. Money is extinguished when loans are repaid. Debt money is therefore an inherently temporary and volatile form of money supply. Source: Congressional Research Service — confirmed primary source.

2.4 Economists

Hyman Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis documented that speculative euphoria leads to debts exceeding repayment capacity — ignored for decades until 2008 confirmed its predictions. PART III: BOND CREATION AND THE MONETARY SYSTEM

3.1 What a Treasury Bond Is

ESTABLISHED:

Treasury securities are backed by the full faith and credit of the United States — the government's promise to pay backed by its authority to tax and borrow further. Not gold — not since 1971. Sold at auction through the Federal Reserve Bank of New York to primary dealers. Source: Treasury Department confirmed.

3.2 The Documented Mechanism

ESTABLISHED:

Congress authorizes deficit spending. Treasury issues bonds. Bonds sold at auction to primary dealers. Federal Reserve buys existing Treasury securities in open market — creating new money as bank reserves at moment of purchase. New money enters banking system where commercial banks create additional money through lending. National debt grows. Source: Federal Reserve FAQ confirmed. Levy Economics Institute confirmed. Treasury confirmed.

3.3 The Self-Referential Nature

SUPPORTED:

The dollar is backed by Treasury bonds. Treasury bonds are backed by the government's promise to pay dollars. The system is self-referential. National debt grew from $398 billion in 1971 to $36 trillion in 2026 — the documented numerical record of this system operating over 55 years. These are documented facts. They form their own conclusion.

PART V: THE BANK FOR INTERNATIONAL SETTLEMENTS

5.1 What the BIS Is

ESTABLISHED:

The Bank for International Settlements is an international financial institution owned by member central banks — 63 central banks with the largest shareholders being G10 central banks including the Federal Reserve. Established in 1930 it is the oldest international financial institution. Its original stated goal was to promote cooperation of central banks and provide additional facilities for international financial operations. Source: BIS Statutes of 1930 confirmed. Wikipedia confirmed.

5.2 How It Was Founded

ESTABLISHED:

The BIS was established in 1930 to facilitate German reparation payments imposed by the Treaty of Versailles. When the reparations system collapsed in the early 1930s the BIS continued — its other functions ensuring its continued relevance beyond its original purpose. Source: BIS official website confirmed. Britannica confirmed.

5.3 The Basel Accords — How the BIS Sets Global Banking Rules

ESTABLISHED:

The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision — secretariat located at the BIS — formulates international banking capital standards known as the Basel Accords. Forty-five bank regulators worldwide including the United States implement these standards. The Basel frameworks have no legal force in the United States — domestic regulators have chosen to closely align their rules with these frameworks. This choice reflects that U.S. bank regulators are influential BCBS members and BCBS standards likely reflect at least in part the views and preferences of the U.S. Source: Congressional Research Service — confirmed primary source.

5.4 The Institutional Hierarchy

SUPPORTED:

The documented chain from 1863 to 2026 shows seven institutional layers: National banks created by Congress 1863. Federal Reserve created by Congress 1913 — privately owned. BIS founded 1930 — owned by central banks including the Federal Reserve. IMF founded 1944 — with U.S. veto power built in. Dollar removed from gold 1971 — system becomes self-referential. ESTABLISHED:

Common law fraud requires: a false representation of a material fact, made with knowledge of its falsity, made with intent to deceive, justifiable reliance by the plaintiff, and resulting damages. Source: Robert D. Mitchell citing Oregon, New Jersey, and Minnesota Supreme Court decisions — confirmed primary legal source.

7.5 The Foreign Minting Constitutional Question — The Dual Standard

This section documents what the primary source record establishes regarding the U.S. Mint striking metal-backed coins for foreign governments during the same period the domestic monetary system was departing from metal-backed currency. It applies the same evidence categorization used throughout this document and the companion research.

ESTABLISHED:

Article I Section 8 Clause 5 of the Constitution grants Congress the power to “coin Money, regulate the Value thereof, and of foreign Coin.” This is the foundational constitutional text governing monetary authority. It was not amended at any point to authorize foreign coining operations. Source: Constitutional text — primary source confirmed. Documented in Section 1.1 of this research.

ESTABLISHED:

In 1874, Congress passed legislation authorizing the Director of the U.S. Mint to strike coins for foreign governments, provided such production did not interfere with domestic coin production. The Philadelphia Mint subsequently struck coins for more than 40 foreign governments from 1876 through the early 1980s. The first foreign coins struck were for Venezuela in 1875 and 1876. Source: U.S. Mint official history — confirmed. Coin Classroom U.S. Mint Timeline — confirmed. U.S. Mint Historic Coin Production — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED:

The coins struck by the Philadelphia Mint for foreign governments during this period were metal-backed — silver and gold — consistent with the hard money standard those governments maintained. During the same period, the United States was progressively departing from metal-backed currency for its domestic population: the Federal Reserve Act passed 1913, the gold standard formally abandoned domestically 1933 under FDR, the Bretton Woods gold-dollar link closed by Nixon August 15 1971 without congressional authorization. The documented timeline of domestic monetary departure runs directly alongside the documented timeline of metal-backed foreign coin production. Source: U.S. Mint history confirmed. Nixon tapes and Section 1.5 of this research confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — MOROCCO SPECIFIC:

Morocco was among the foreign governments for which the U.S. Mint struck coins during this period. The 1786 Treaty of Peace and Friendship between the United States and Morocco — documented in companion research Judgement of No Legal Accountability v26, Part XVI — established the commercial relationship under which this coining cooperation occurred. Sultan Mohammed III had maintained a silver-based monetary system at the time of the treaty. The coining relationship continued that metal-backed framework decades after the U.S. had begun departing from it domestically. Source: U.S. Mint foreign coin production record confirmed. Morocco-U.S. treaty relationship documented in companion research v26.

SUPPORTED:

The documented record shows the U.S. government operating simultaneously under two monetary standards during the same historical period — metal-backed for foreign governments served by the Mint, debt-based fiat for its domestic population. Both operations occurred under the same Article I Section 8 constitutional authority. The constitutional question of whether Congress could authorize the Mint to produce metal-backed currency for foreign sovereigns while removing that same standard from the domestic population — without constitutional amendment — has never been formally examined by any court. This fits the pattern documented throughout this research and in the companion research: constitutional text authorizing one framework, institutional practice operating under another, the gap never formally resolved. The argument is sound. Primary source confirmation of the dual operation is established. The constitutional implication is supported, not established.

7.6 The Dual Standard Upgraded — ESTABLISHED

Section 7.5 documented the dual standard as SUPPORTED pending a specific confirmed example of metal-backed foreign coinage continuing after the domestic gold standard was removed. That specific confirmation now exists in the primary source record and upgrades the dual standard to ESTABLISHED.

ESTABLISHED — THE UPGRADE EVIDENCE:

In 1944 and 1945 — eleven years after the United States removed the domestic gold standard (1933) and twenty-seven years after the Federal Reserve Act (1913) — the Philadelphia Mint struck 91,210 gold discs for the Arabian American Oil Company (Aramco) to pay the Saudi Royal Government $3 million per year in oil royalties. These discs were equivalent in value and gold content to the $20 gold double eagle. The obverse depicted the heraldic U.S. eagle with the legend “U.S. Mint, Philadelphia, USA.” They are documented as the only gold coins struck by the U.S. Mint for foreign countries during the entire Second World War. Source: CoinWeek, Foreign Coins Struck by the United States Mint: The Mint in World War II — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — THE DUAL STANDARD:

The documented timeline now confirms the dual standard operating simultaneously under the same constitutional authority. In 1933 the U.S. government removed gold coin from domestic circulation under Executive Order 6102 — requiring American citizens to exchange gold coins and bullion for Federal Reserve Notes. In 1944 the same government’s Mint struck gold discs equivalent to $20 gold eagles for a foreign sovereign. The domestic population had been legally prohibited from holding the same gold standard the Mint was providing to foreign governments. Both operations occurred under the same Article I Section 8 authority. The constitutional question of whether this dual operation was authorized without amendment has never been formally examined by any court. Source: Executive Order 6102 (1933) — primary source confirmed. CoinWeek Mint WWII foreign coinage — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — THE SCALE:

The U.S. Mint struck metal-backed coins for 43 foreign governments between 1876 and the early 1980s — a period spanning the Federal Reserve Act (1913), the domestic gold standard removal (1933), the Bretton Woods collapse (1971), and the complete shift to fiat currency for the domestic population. Throughout that entire period the Mint maintained the technical capacity and legal authority to produce metal-backed coinage. That capacity was extended to foreign sovereigns while being progressively withdrawn from the domestic population without constitutional amendment. Source: U.S. Mint official records — confirmed. CoinWeek foreign coinage records — confirmed. Documented timeline cross-referenced with Section 1.5 of this research.

7.7 Specific Overlaps — Coining Partner and Debt Enforcement in the Same Nations

7.7.1 Venezuela — The Sharpest Documented Overlap

ESTABLISHED — THE COINING RELATIONSHIP:

In 1875 and 1876, the Philadelphia Mint struck the first foreign coins authorized under the 1874 Act — two denominations for the Republic of Venezuela: 2 million 2½ centavo and 10 million 1 centavo copper-nickel coins. Venezuela was the inaugural foreign client of the U.S. Mint under the 1874 congressional authorization. Source: U.S. Mint official history — confirmed. U.S. Mint Historic Coin Production — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — THE DEBT ENFORCEMENT:

In December 1902 — twenty-six years after the Philadelphia Mint struck Venezuela’s inaugural coins — Britain, Germany, and Italy imposed a naval blockade on Venezuela after President Cipriano Castro refused to pay foreign debts. The United States was officially neutral and allowed the action to proceed without objection. By January 1903 the blockade had devastated Venezuela’s economy. Venezuela agreed to reserve 30% of its customs duties until all debt claims were settled. Source: Wikipedia, Venezuelan Crisis of 1902–1903 — confirmed. Britannica, Roosevelt Corollary — confirmed. Theodore Roosevelt Center — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — U.S. TACIT APPROVAL:

President Roosevelt and his State Department interpreted the Monroe Doctrine as applying only to European seizure of territory, not to military intervention per se. With prior assurances that no territorial seizure would occur, the U.S. gave the blockade tacit approval. Roosevelt subsequently used the crisis to formulate the Roosevelt Corollary (1904) — asserting the U.S. right to intervene in Latin American affairs to maintain regional stability and ensure debt repayment. Source: Wikipedia, Venezuelan Crisis of 1902–1903 — confirmed. Britannica, Roosevelt Corollary — confirmed.

SUPPORTED — THE DOCUMENTED PATTERN:

The U.S. government provided metal-backed coinage to Venezuela in 1876 as the inaugural act of its new commercial minting authority. Twenty-six years later the same government stood aside while European powers used a naval blockade to enforce debt collection against the same country. The coining relationship and the debt enforcement relationship operated through the same institutional framework — the Treasury and its commercial partners — without any documented acknowledgment of the connection between them. Whether any Treasury, State Department, or congressional record from 1902–1904 referenced the prior coining relationship with Venezuela in the context of the debt crisis requires review. The argument that these represent two faces of the same institutional dual standard is sound. The primary sources confirm both operations independently. The connection between them is SUPPORTED, not ESTABLISHED, pending review of the official record from that period.

7.7.2 China — The Mirror Image

ESTABLISHED — THE COINING RELATIONSHIP:

Approximately 1935, the San Francisco Federal Mint struck more than 10 million coins for the Chinese government over a two-month period. Employees worked behind bullet-proof enclosures during production. This is documented in National Archives holdings at the San Francisco facility and confirmed in National Archives Foundation records describing the Mint’s 1930s production for China during a silver and copper alloy shortage. Source: National Archives San Francisco — confirmed. National Archives Foundation — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — THE SIMULTANEOUS DOMESTIC DEPARTURE:

In 1935 the Chinese Nationalist government nationalized its major banks and in 1936 established a fiat currency system — transitioning away from the silver standard at the same moment the U.S. Mint was striking silver and copper coins for China. The U.S. domestic gold standard had been removed in 1933 under Executive Order 6102. In 1935 the U.S. Mint was providing metal-backed coins to a foreign government that was simultaneously transitioning to fiat — while the U.S. domestic population had already been moved off the gold standard two years earlier. Source: CEPR, Financial Revolution in Republican China 1900–1937 — confirmed. Executive Order 6102 — primary source confirmed.

SUPPORTED — THE MIRROR IMAGE:

The China 1935 example presents a mirror image of the domestic U.S. situation. The U.S. government removed its own citizens from the gold standard in 1933. Two years later it was providing metal-backed coins to a foreign government that was itself transitioning away from metal backing. Both governments moved their domestic populations toward fiat in the same era. The U.S. Mint served as a metal-backed coining contractor for both during the transition period. The institutional capacity for hard asset coinage was maintained commercially while being withdrawn from both domestic populations through policy. Whether any Treasury document from 1935 acknowledged this parallel is an open research question. The pattern is supported by the documented facts on both sides.

WHAT THE RECORD SHOWS:

7.8.2 Argentina 1919–1920 — Treasury Document No. 2874 Confirmed

ESTABLISHED — SPECIFIC PRODUCTION:

The 1920 Annual Report coinage table confirms that the Philadelphia Mint struck 10-centavo planchets and 5-centavo planchets for Argentina during the fiscal year ended June 30, 1920, totaling over 16 million pieces across both denominations. Source: Treasury Document No. 2874, coinage table — primary source confirmed, Internet Archive full text verified.

Error coins from this period (Lincoln cents struck on Argentina 10-centavo planchets) document the presence of Argentine planchets in the Philadelphia Mint production environment. 7.13 The 1873/1874 Chronological Sequence — What the Record Shows

ESTABLISHED — THE COINAGE ACT OF 1873:

The Coinage Act of 1873, signed by President Grant on February 12, 1873, ended the right of holders of silver bullion to have it coined into standard silver dollars, while allowing holders of gold to continue to have their bullion made into money. This effectively moved the United States from bimetallism toward the gold standard. The ramifications were not immediately noticed — silver prices remained high until new western mines increased supply and drove prices down. When silver producers sought to present silver at the mints for coining, they discovered the right had been removed. The Act became known as the “Crime of 1873.” Source: U.S. Mint official history, Crime of 1873 — confirmed. Wikipedia, Coinage Act of 1873 — confirmed. EBSCO Research Starters — confirmed.
