### AFFIDAVIT  (noun)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

A voluntary declaration of facts written down and sworn to by the declarant before an officer authorized to administer oaths. A written statement confirmed by oath or affirmation, for use as evidence in court. **EMBEDDED ASSUMPTION**

The dominant definition of affidavit presents it as a formal legal instrument whose value derives from the institutional apparatus that administers and records it — the notary, the court seal, the official filing. The Declaration of Standing in the Odyssey-1 system is an affidavit in this fullest sense: a solemn declaration of the living being's identity, standing, and rights, made under oath to the Creator whose jurisdiction over truth the system's own judicial oaths invoke.

### PROCEDURAL DEFAULT  (noun phrase)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

The failure to raise a claim or objection at the proper time and in the proper manner in the lower court, resulting in the forfeiture of the right to raise it on appeal. A claim that was not properly preserved at trial is procedurally defaulted and generally cannot be raised for the first time on appeal absent plain error or ineffective assistance of counsel. (Black's Law Dictionary, 11th ed.)

**EMBEDDED ASSUMPTION**

Procedural default is presented as a neutral procedural rule ensuring judicial efficiency — issues must be raised at the trial level so the trial court has an opportunity to correct them. In practice it is one of the most powerful mechanisms by which the enterprise shields its own violations from review. An attorney's failure to object — whether through incompetence, divided loyalty, excessive caseload, or simple error — permanently bars the living being from raising a legitimate constitutional violation on appeal. The living being is punished for their attorney's failure. The constitutional violation is immunized from review. The institution that committed the violation benefits from the procedural barrier that its own complex rules make easy to trigger and nearly impossible to overcome.

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

Procedural default is a rule that the enterprise uses to protect its own violations from review by converting the attorney's failure into the living being's permanent forfeiture. The exceptions to procedural default — actual innocence, cause and prejudice, ineffective assistance of counsel — are the law's partial acknowledgment that the rule produces unjust results. Martinez v. Ryan, 566 U.S. 1 (2012), established that ineffective assistance of post-conviction counsel can excuse procedural default of an ineffective assistance of trial counsel claim — recognizing that the cascade of attorney failures that procedural default protects cannot be used to permanently foreclose review of genuine constitutional violations. In international forums, procedural default does not operate. The IACHR and the UN Human Rights Committee evaluate whether the living being's rights were violated — not whether their attorney preserved the issue correctly. The procedural barriers the domestic system built to protect its violations do not travel to the international forum.

**Authority:** Martinez v. Ryan, 566 U.S. 1 (2012): ineffective assistance as cause to excuse procedural default. Coleman v. Thompson, 501 U.S. 722 (1991): procedural default standard. Schlup v. Delo, 513 U.S. 298 (1995): actual innocence gateway. IACHR Rules of Procedure: procedural default does not bar international petition on rights violations.

**Practical Application:** When an issue has been procedurally defaulted, assess immediately whether any exception applies: actual innocence, cause and prejudice, ineffective assistance at any level. If no domestic exception is available, the international forum does not recognize the default. File the IACHR petition on the merits of the rights violation regardless of the domestic procedural bar.

**THE TRUTH:** The rule that says you cannot raise what your attorney failed to raise is a rule that protects the system's violations behind the attorney's failures. The Creator's court does not apply procedural default. The violation is heard.

### DEPOSITION  (noun)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

The testimony of a witness taken under oath outside of court, reduced to writing, and used as evidence in court. Depositions are part of the pretrial discovery process and allow parties to obtain testimony from witnesses and parties before trial. The deponent is subject to cross-examination during the deposition. (Black's Law Dictionary, 11th ed.)

**EMBEDDED ASSUMPTION**

The dominant treatment of depositions presents them as neutral discovery tools — opportunities for both sides to learn what witnesses know before trial. In practice, depositions are expensive, controlled by attorneys, and often used as much to intimidate witnesses and lock them into statements as to discover the truth. The living being deposed by a skilled opposing attorney — asked carefully constructed questions designed to elicit answers that can be used against them rather than to discover what they actually know — faces a proceeding that is procedurally formal but substantively adversarial in the extreme.

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 30(e) gives the deponent the right to review and make changes to the transcript within 30 days. The deposition that captures the living being's complete and accurate account of what happened — made under oath, preserved in transcript, admissible in every subsequent proceeding — is one of the most powerful record-building tools available.

**Authority:** Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 30: depositions generally. FRCP 30(e): deponent's right to review and correct transcript. FRCP 30(c)(2): objections during deposition. Federal Rule of Evidence 804: deposition as former testimony when witness is unavailable.

**Practical Application:** In any deposition, answer truthfully and completely. Do not guess. Do not speculate. Do not answer questions you do not understand — ask for clarification. Review the transcript carefully within the 30-day window and correct any transcription errors. The deposition record that accurately captures what you know and what happened is a permanent asset in every forum where the matter is subsequently raised.

### HEARSAY  (noun)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

An out-of-court statement offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted. Hearsay is generally inadmissible under Federal Rule of Evidence 802 because the declarant is not subject to cross-examination and the statement's reliability cannot be tested. Numerous exceptions to the hearsay rule exist where the circumstances of the statement provide adequate guarantees of trustworthiness. (Black's Law Dictionary, 11th ed.; Federal Rule of Evidence 801-807)

**EMBEDDED ASSUMPTION**

The hearsay rule is presented as a neutral evidentiary principle designed to ensure that only reliable evidence reaches the fact-finder. In practice, the hearsay rule and its many exceptions have been developed by courts — the same courts whose institutional interests are served by certain evidence being admitted and certain evidence being excluded. The exceptions to hearsay — excited utterance, present sense impression, business records, public records — tend to favor institutional actors whose documents fall within the exceptions over individual living beings whose out-of-court statements do not. A police officer's report is a business record admitted under an exception. The living being's statement to a friend about what the officer did to them is hearsay excluded under the rule.

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

Hearsay is a category of evidence the system has decided it does not trust — but the system's decisions about what to trust reflect the same institutional biases documented throughout the Counter-Canon. The living being who understands the hearsay rule can use it strategically: knowing which exceptions apply to which statements, knowing how to frame the living being's own out-of-court statements to fall within available exceptions, and knowing how to challenge the admission of hearsay that favors the opposing party. Federal Rule of Evidence 807 — the residual exception — permits admission of hearsay not covered by a specific exception when it has equivalent circumstantial guarantees of trustworthiness and admission serves the interests of justice. This residual exception is the living being's opening to argue that evidence of what was done to them — evidence the system would otherwise exclude — deserves admission because justice requires it.

**Authority:** Federal Rules of Evidence 801-807: hearsay and exceptions. FRE 807: residual exception. FRE 803(6): business records exception. FRE 803(8): public records exception. Crawford v. Washington, 541 U.S. 36 (2004): confrontation clause limits on hearsay.

**Practical Application:** When evidence of what was done to you is excluded as hearsay, identify which exception might apply and argue for admission. When the opposing party's hearsay is admitted under an exception, challenge the exception's applicability. The hearsay rule is not a wall — it is a door with many keys. Know the keys.

**THE TRUTH:** What you told your family about what was done to you is true whether or not the court admits it. The rules of evidence determine what the system will hear. They do not determine what happened.
