## IV. CONSTITUTIONAL PILLARS

### A. First Amendment: Freedom of Religion and Conscience

The First Amendment prohibits Congress from making any law respecting an establishment of religion or prohibiting the free exercise thereof. Church of Lukumi Babalu Aye v. City of Hialeah, 508 U.S. 520 (1993), affirmed that laws targeting specific religious practices are subject to strict scrutiny and must serve a compelling governmental interest by the least restrictive means. Any government action that treats the claimant's spiritual being as a commercial unit without acknowledging its sacred nature implicates this Amendment.

### B. Fourth Amendment: Security of the Person

The Fourth Amendment guarantees the right of the people to be secure in their persons against unreasonable searches and seizures. Terry v. Ohio, 392 U.S. 1 (1968), requires reasonable articulable suspicion specific to the individual and not suspicion based on race or demographic profile. Florida v. Bostick, 501 U.S. 429 (1991), established that a person not free to leave is seized within the meaning of the Fourth Amendment. Any detention not supported by individualized, articulable, race-neutral suspicion violates this pillar.

### C. Fifth Amendment: Due Process

The Fifth Amendment protects the living being from deprivation of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319 (1976), established the balancing test for procedural due process, weighing private interest, risk of erroneous deprivation, and governmental interest. Where the private interest is the spiritual being itself, its dignity, ancestral identity, and sovereignty, that interest is among the highest cognizable by any court.

### D. Thirteenth Amendment: Badges and Incidents of Slavery

The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery and granted Congress power to enforce it by appropriate legislation. Jones v. Alfred H. Mayer Co., 392 U.S. 409 (1968), held that Congress has the power to determine what are the badges and incidents of slavery and to translate that determination into effective legislation, extending the Amendment beyond formal chattel slavery to its lingering effects in American society and law.

### E. Fourteenth Amendment: Equal Protection

The Fourteenth Amendment guarantees equal protection of the laws to all persons. Washington v. Davis, 426 U.S. 229 (1976), held that facially neutral laws with racially disparate impact may constitute equal protection violations when discriminatory purpose is shown. The documented pattern of racially disparate enforcement in policing, prosecution, and sentencing constitutes the evidentiary foundation for this claim.

### F. Georgia Constitutional Provisions

The Georgia Constitution, Article I, Section I, Paragraph I guarantees no deprivation of life, liberty, or property except by due process. Paragraph II protects against unreasonable searches and seizures. Paragraph IV protects freedom of religion and provides that no inhabitant shall be molested in person or property on account of religious opinions. These provisions apply independently of and in addition to federal constitutional protections, providing a second layer of defense in any Georgia state forum.

## V. THE RFRA ARGUMENT: SPIRITUAL EXISTENCE AS PROTECTED BELIEF

The Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993, 42 U.S.C. section 2000bb et seq., prohibits the federal government from substantially burdening a person's exercise of religion even by a rule of general applicability unless the government demonstrates a compelling governmental interest pursued by the least restrictive means. The Georgia Religious Freedom Restoration Act, O.C.G.A. section 50-3-1 et seq., imposes the same standard on the Georgia state government and its agencies.

### A. Sincerity of Belief

The spiritual framework of this doctrine is sincerely held and documented across seven volumes of theological, philosophical, and scientific scholarship. Courts do not evaluate the objective truth of religious belief, only its sincerity. Thomas v. Review Board, 450 U.S. 707 (1981). The sincerity of this belief is established by the depth, consistency, and documentary record of the claimant's spiritual practice spanning multiple years of continuous development, including the Unified Bio-Cosmic Generator Model, the Sacred Geometry framework, and the seven volumes of The Sovereign Self: Reclaiming Divine Intent in Law and Governance.

### B. Substantial Burden

Any government action that treats the living being as a commercial unit, a taxable entity, a manageable liability, or a source of extractable labor, without acknowledging the sacred nature of that being as defined by the claimant's sincerely held belief, constitutes a substantial burden on the exercise of that religion. Burwell v. Hobby Lobby Stores, Inc., 573 U.S. 682 (2014), confirmed that RFRA's protections extend broadly and that the government bears the burden of demonstrating compelling interest and least restrictive means once substantial burden is shown.

### C. The Government's Burden: Compelling Interest and Least Restrictive Means

Once substantial burden on sincere religious belief is established, the government must produce specific evidence of compelling interest pursued by least restrictive means. Gonzales v. O Centro Espirita Beneficente Uniao do Vegetal, 546 U.S. 418 (2006), held that RFRA requires the government to demonstrate compelling interest in the specific case and not merely in the abstract. Where the government cannot demonstrate that treating the living being's spiritual existence as a commercial unit is the least restrictive means of achieving any compelling interest, the RFRA claim succeeds.

## VIII. PHYSICAL LIBERTY AND UNLAWFUL DETENTION

The physical liberty of the living being is among the most fundamental rights protected by the Constitution. Any interference with that liberty by an agent of the state must satisfy strict constitutional requirements.

### A. The Constitutional Standard for Stops and Detention

A police officer may conduct a brief investigative stop only when supported by reasonable articulable suspicion that the person has been, is, or is about to be engaged in criminal activity. Terry v. Ohio, 392 U.S. 1 (1968). That suspicion must be based on specific, articulable facts about the individual and not on race, neighborhood, or generalized profile. United States v. Sokolow, 490 U.S. 1 (1989). A person is seized within the meaning of the Fourth Amendment when their freedom of movement is restrained by physical force or a show of authority. Florida v. Bostick, 501 U.S. 429 (1991). An arrest requires probable cause, a substantially higher standard. Dunaway v. New York, 442 U.S. 200 (1979).

### B. Civil Rights Violations Under Color of Law

18 U.S.C. section 241 makes it a federal crime for two or more persons to conspire to injure, oppress, threaten, or intimidate any person in the free exercise of any right secured by the Constitution or laws of the United States. 18 U.S.C. section 242 makes it a federal crime for any person acting under color of any law to willfully deprive any person of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured or protected by the Constitution or laws of the United States. 42 U.S.C. section 1983 provides the civil remedy for any person acting under color of state law who violates these rights.

### C. Qualified Immunity and Its Limits

The doctrine of qualified immunity shields government officials from civil liability unless their conduct violates clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known. Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U.S. 800 (1982). Where constitutional rights are clearly established, as they are in the context of racially motivated stops, excessive force, and unlawful detention, qualified immunity does not apply. Pearson v. Callahan, 555 U.S. 223 (2009). This doctrine relies on section 1983 against state actors for civil remedy, which remains fully available.

## XII. LOPER BRIGHT AND THE END OF AGENCY DEFERENCE

In Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo, 603 U.S. ___ (2024), the Supreme Court overruled Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, 467 U.S. 837 (1984), eliminating the doctrine that required courts to defer to federal agencies' interpretations of ambiguous statutes. This decision creates significant new openings for the arguments advanced in this doctrine.

### A. What Loper Bright Means for the Average Person

For forty years, Chevron deference meant that when a federal agency interpreted an ambiguous statute in a way that burdened individuals, courts would defer to that interpretation. Loper Bright ends that deference. Courts must now independently interpret statutes, which means that agency overreach is no longer protected by automatic judicial deference. Any agency action that rests on an aggressive interpretation of statutory authority is now vulnerable to independent judicial scrutiny.

### B. Application to Claims Under This Doctrine

IRS regulations, administrative enforcement actions, agency licensing requirements, and federal program eligibility rules that have been applied to burden the living being's religious practice, economic sovereignty, or physical liberty, and that rest on agency interpretation of ambiguous statutory language, are now subject to independent judicial review. Every argument in this doctrine that involves agency action should include a Loper Bright challenge to the statutory authority underlying that action.

The following framework translates this doctrine into operative defenses for specific real-world encounters. Each defense is designed to be non-frivolous, legally grounded, and usable by a living being without institutional legal resources.

### A. Upon Any Encounter with Law Enforcement

- The right to know whether you are free to leave. If the officer says no, you are seized and the Fourth Amendment applies in full.
- The right to remain silent. Fifth Amendment. Invoke it explicitly: *I am invoking my right to remain silent.*
- The right to refuse consent to search. The officer may search without consent only with a warrant or a recognized exception.
- The right to record the encounter in any public space. First Amendment. Glik v. Cunniffe, 655 F.3d 78 (1st Cir. 2011).
- The right to be free from racially motivated stops. Any stop based on race rather than individualized suspicion violates the Fourth and Fourteenth Amendments.

### B. Upon Any Administrative or Agency Action

The following challenges are available:

- **RFRA challenge:** Does this agency action substantially burden a sincerely held religious belief? If so, the government must demonstrate compelling interest and least restrictive means.
- **Loper Bright challenge:** Does the agency's action rest on an interpretation of ambiguous statutory language? If so, that interpretation is no longer entitled to deference.
- **Due process challenge:** Has the agency provided adequate notice and an opportunity to be heard before depriving the living being of any right or interest? Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319 (1976).
- **Equal protection challenge:** Is this agency action applied with racially disparate effect and identifiable discriminatory purpose?

### C. Upon Any Court Proceeding

The following foundational assertions establish the living being's standing:

- This court operates under an oath to God and truth, which this doctrine acknowledges and holds the court to.
- This living being's existence precedes the State. The State's jurisdiction extends to conduct and not to the ontological nature of the being.
- This living being asserts all rights under the First, Fourth, Fifth, Thirteenth, and Fourteenth Amendments; under RFRA; under the Georgia Constitution; and under applicable international human rights instruments.
