### The 13th Amendment and the Punishment Exception

The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery and involuntary servitude except as punishment for crime. That exception was immediately exploited. Within months of ratification, the former Confederate states enacted the Black Codes — laws that made it a criminal offense to be unemployed, to be without a labor contract, to be present in certain places without permission, to speak in ways that offended white authority.

**EVIDENCE:** Black Codes of Mississippi, 1865 — *Any freedman found with no lawful employment or business shall be deemed a vagrant and fined fifty dollars or imprisoned at hard labor for not exceeding ten days.* Vagrancy — the condition of being free and unemployed — was a crime. The crime triggered the punishment exception. The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery on November 6, 1865. The Mississippi Black Codes were enacted on November 24, 1865. **Eighteen days.**

Convict leasing followed. States leased convicted persons — overwhelmingly Black men convicted under the Black Codes — to private corporations and plantations for labor. The state received revenue. The corporation received free labor. The person received nothing. The Thirteenth Amendment's punishment exception was the legal mechanism. The enterprise continued under a different name.

**EVIDENCE:** *The Civil Rights Cases*, 109 U.S. 3 (1883) — The Supreme Court struck down the Civil Rights Act of 1875, which had prohibited racial discrimination in public accommodations.
