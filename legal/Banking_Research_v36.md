PART I: THE CONSTITUTIONAL AUTHORITY CHAIN

1.1 Constitutional Basis

ESTABLISHED:

Article I Section 8 grants Congress the power to coin money and regulate its value. Neither provision was amended to authorize the Federal Reserve Act of 1913 or the delegation of monetary authority to a private banking system. That constitutional question has never been definitively resolved by the Supreme Court. Source: Constitutional text confirmed. Companion research Part IV confirmed.

1.2 National Banking Acts 1863 and 1864

ESTABLISHED:

The National Bank Act of 1863 was passed by a 23 to 21 Senate vote. It tied banking directly to government debt from the foundation — banks could issue notes only proportional to government bonds held. It established the OCC. National banks created were entirely privately owned. Source: Federal Reserve History confirmed.

1.3 Federal Reserve Act 1913

ESTABLISHED:

Passed December 23 1913 when approximately 30 senators had left. HR 2990 112th Congress stated: In 1913 Congress effectively delegated the sovereign power to create money to the Federal Reserve system and private financial industry. Source: Senate record confirmed. HR 2990 confirmed.

1.4 The 1930s Depression Response

ESTABLISHED:

The Federal Reserve's own historical record documents its failure during the Depression. The Banking Act of 1935 centralized monetary control in a seven-member Board of Governors — moving authority further from regional banks. Source: Federal Reserve History confirmed.

1.5 Nixon Decision 1971

ESTABLISHED:

Nixon closed the gold window August 15 1971 without congressional authorization — deliberately timed to avoid Congress per his own archived tapes. Federal debt grew from $398 billion to over $36 trillion. Middle-income households declined from 61% to 51% of Americans. Source: Federal Reserve History confirmed. Nixon tapes confirmed. Fraser Institute confirmed. Pew Research confirmed.

PART II: HOW MONEY IS CREATED IN A DEBT-BASED SYSTEM

2.1 The Bank of England Primary Source

ESTABLISHED:

The majority of money in the modern economy is created by commercial banks making loans. Banks do not act simply as intermediaries lending out deposits that savers place with them. Source: Bank of England Quarterly Bulletin 2014 Q1 — McLeay, Radia, and Thomas — confirmed primary source.

2.2 The Mechanism

ESTABLISHED:

Whenever a bank makes a loan it creates a deposit in the borrower's bank account thereby creating new money. 92% of deposits in the banking system resulted from lending activity — only 8% from cash deposits. Source: Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia confirmed.

2.3 Congressional Research Service

ESTABLISHED:

John B. Henderson, Senior Specialist in Price Economics, Congressional Research Service: Money is created when loans are issued and debts incurred. Money is extinguished when loans are repaid. Debt money is therefore an inherently temporary and volatile form of money supply. Source: Congressional Research Service — confirmed primary source.

2.4 Economists

PART III: BOND CREATION AND THE MONETARY SYSTEM

3.1 What a Treasury Bond Is

ESTABLISHED:

Treasury securities are backed by the full faith and credit of the United States — the government's promise to pay backed by its authority to tax and borrow further. Not gold — not since 1971. Sold at auction through the Federal Reserve Bank of New York to primary dealers. Source: Treasury Department confirmed.

3.2 The Documented Mechanism

ESTABLISHED:

Congress authorizes deficit spending. Treasury issues bonds. Bonds sold at auction to primary dealers. Federal Reserve buys existing Treasury securities in open market — creating new money as bank reserves at moment of purchase. New money enters banking system where commercial banks create additional money through lending. National debt grows. Source: Federal Reserve FAQ confirmed. Levy Economics Institute confirmed. Treasury confirmed.

3.3 The Self-Referential Nature

SUPPORTED:

The dollar is backed by Treasury bonds. Treasury bonds are backed by the government's promise to pay dollars. The system is self-referential. National debt grew from $398 billion in 1971 to $36 trillion in 2026 — the documented numerical record of this system operating over 55 years. These are documented facts. They form their own conclusion.

PART V: THE BANK FOR INTERNATIONAL SETTLEMENTS

5.1 What the BIS Is

ESTABLISHED:

The Bank for International Settlements is an international financial institution owned by member central banks — 63 central banks with the largest shareholders being G10 central banks including the Federal Reserve. Established in 1930 it is the oldest international financial institution. Its original stated goal was to promote cooperation of central banks and provide additional facilities for international financial operations. Source: BIS Statutes of 1930 confirmed. Wikipedia confirmed.

5.2 How It Was Founded

ESTABLISHED:

The BIS was established in 1930 to facilitate German reparation payments imposed by the Treaty of Versailles. When the reparations system collapsed in the early 1930s the BIS continued — its other functions ensuring its continued relevance beyond its original purpose. Source: BIS official website confirmed. Britannica confirmed.

5.3 The Basel Accords — How the BIS Sets Global Banking Rules

ESTABLISHED:

The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision — secretariat located at the BIS — formulates international banking capital standards known as the Basel Accords. Forty-five bank regulators worldwide including the United States implement these standards. The Basel frameworks have no legal force in the United States — domestic regulators have chosen to closely align their rules with these frameworks. This choice reflects that U.S. bank regulators are influential BCBS members and BCBS standards likely reflect at least in part the views and preferences of the U.S. Source: Congressional Research Service — confirmed primary source.

ESTABLISHED:

Common law fraud requires: a false representation of a material fact, made with knowledge of its falsity, made with intent to deceive, justifiable reliance by the plaintiff, and resulting damages. Source: Robert D. Mitchell citing Oregon, New Jersey, and Minnesota Supreme Court decisions — confirmed primary legal source.

7.5 The Foreign Minting Constitutional Question — The Dual Standard

This section documents what the primary source record establishes regarding the U.S. Mint striking metal-backed coins for foreign governments during the same period the domestic monetary system was departing from metal-backed currency. It applies the same evidence categorization used throughout this document and the companion research.

ESTABLISHED:

Article I Section 8 Clause 5 of the Constitution grants Congress the power to “coin Money, regulate the Value thereof, and of foreign Coin.” This is the foundational constitutional text governing monetary authority. It was not amended at any point to authorize foreign coining operations. Source: Constitutional text — primary source confirmed. Documented in Section 1.1 of this research.

ESTABLISHED:

In 1874, Congress passed legislation authorizing the Director of the U.S. Mint to strike coins for foreign governments, provided such production did not interfere with domestic coin production. The Philadelphia Mint subsequently struck coins for more than 40 foreign governments from 1876 through the early 1980s. The first foreign coins struck were for Venezuela in 1875 and 1876. Source: U.S. Mint official history — confirmed. Coin Classroom U.S. Mint Timeline — confirmed. U.S. Mint Historic Coin Production — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED:

The coins struck by the Philadelphia Mint for foreign governments during this period were metal-backed — silver and gold — consistent with the hard money standard those governments maintained. During the same period, the United States was progressively departing from metal-backed currency for its domestic population: the Federal Reserve Act passed 1913, the gold standard formally abandoned domestically 1933 under FDR, the Bretton Woods gold-dollar link closed by Nixon August 15 1971 without congressional authorization. The documented timeline of domestic monetary departure runs directly alongside the documented timeline of metal-backed foreign coin production. Source: U.S. Mint history confirmed. Nixon tapes and Section 1.5 of this research confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — MOROCCO SPECIFIC:

Morocco was among the foreign governments for which the U.S. Mint struck coins during this period. The 1786 Treaty of Peace and Friendship between the United States and Morocco — documented in companion research Judgement of No Legal Accountability v26, Part XVI — established the commercial relationship under which this coining cooperation occurred. Sultan Mohammed III had maintained a silver-based monetary system at the time of the treaty. The coining relationship continued that metal-backed framework decades after the U.S. had begun departing from it domestically. Source: U.S. Mint foreign coin production record confirmed. Morocco-U.S. treaty relationship documented in companion research v26.

SUPPORTED:

The documented record shows the U.S. government operating simultaneously under two monetary standards during the same historical period — metal-backed for foreign governments served by the Mint, debt-based fiat for its domestic population. Both operations occurred under the same Article I Section 8 constitutional authority. The constitutional question of whether Congress could authorize the Mint to produce metal-backed currency for foreign sovereigns while removing that same standard from the domestic population — without constitutional amendment — has never been formally examined by any court. This fits the pattern documented throughout this research and in the companion research: constitutional text authorizing one framework, institutional practice operating under another, the gap never formally resolved. The argument is sound. Primary source confirmation of the dual operation is established. The constitutional implication is supported, not established.

7.6 The Dual Standard Upgraded — ESTABLISHED

Section 7.5 documented the dual standard as SUPPORTED pending a specific confirmed example of metal-backed foreign coinage continuing after the domestic gold standard was removed. That specific confirmation now exists in the primary source record and upgrades the dual standard to ESTABLISHED.

ESTABLISHED — THE UPGRADE EVIDENCE:

In 1944 and 1945 — eleven years after the United States removed the domestic gold standard (1933) and twenty-seven years after the Federal Reserve Act (1913) — the Philadelphia Mint struck 91,210 gold discs for the Arabian American Oil Company (Aramco) to pay the Saudi Royal Government $3 million per year in oil royalties. These discs were equivalent in value and gold content to the $20 gold double eagle. The obverse depicted the heraldic U.S. eagle with the legend “U.S. Mint, Philadelphia, USA.” They are documented as the only gold coins struck by the U.S. Mint for foreign countries during the entire Second World War. Source: CoinWeek, Foreign Coins Struck by the United States Mint: The Mint in World War II — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — THE DUAL STANDARD:

The documented timeline now confirms the dual standard operating simultaneously under the same constitutional authority. In 1933 the U.S. government removed gold coin from domestic circulation under Executive Order 6102 — requiring American citizens to exchange gold coins and bullion for Federal Reserve Notes. In 1944 the same government’s Mint struck gold discs equivalent to $20 gold eagles for a foreign sovereign. The domestic population had been legally prohibited from holding the same gold standard the Mint was providing to foreign governments. Both operations occurred under the same Article I Section 8 authority. The constitutional question of whether this dual operation was authorized without amendment has never been formally examined by any court. Source: Executive Order 6102 (1933) — primary source confirmed. CoinWeek Mint WWII foreign coinage — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — THE SCALE:

The U.S. Mint struck metal-backed coins for 43 foreign governments between 1876 and the early 1980s — a period spanning the Federal Reserve Act (1913), the domestic gold standard removal (1933), the Bretton Woods collapse (1971), and the complete shift to fiat currency for the domestic population. Throughout that entire period the Mint maintained the technical capacity and legal authority to produce metal-backed coinage. That capacity was extended to foreign sovereigns while being progressively withdrawn from the domestic population without constitutional amendment. Source: U.S. Mint official records — confirmed. CoinWeek foreign coinage records — confirmed. Documented timeline cross-referenced with Section 1.5 of this research.

7.7 Specific Overlaps — Coining Partner and Debt Enforcement in the Same Nations

7.7.1 Venezuela — The Sharpest Documented Overlap

ESTABLISHED — THE COINING RELATIONSHIP:

In 1875 and 1876, the Philadelphia Mint struck the first foreign coins authorized under the 1874 Act — two denominations for the Republic of Venezuela: 2 million 2½ centavo and 10 million 1 centavo copper-nickel coins. Venezuela was the inaugural foreign client of the U.S. Mint under the 1874 congressional authorization. Source: U.S. Mint official history — confirmed. U.S. Mint Historic Coin Production — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — THE DEBT ENFORCEMENT:

In December 1902 — twenty-six years after the Philadelphia Mint struck Venezuela’s inaugural coins — Britain, Germany, and Italy imposed a naval blockade on Venezuela after President Cipriano Castro refused to pay foreign debts. The United States was officially neutral and allowed the action to proceed without objection. By January 1903 the blockade had devastated Venezuela’s economy. Venezuela agreed to reserve 30% of its customs duties until all debt claims were settled. Source: Wikipedia, Venezuelan Crisis of 1902–1903 — confirmed. Britannica, Roosevelt Corollary — confirmed. Theodore Roosevelt Center — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — U.S. TACIT APPROVAL:

President Roosevelt and his State Department interpreted the Monroe Doctrine as applying only to European seizure of territory, not to military intervention per se. With prior assurances that no territorial seizure would occur, the U.S. gave the blockade tacit approval. Roosevelt subsequently used the crisis to formulate the Roosevelt Corollary (1904) — asserting the U.S. right to intervene in Latin American affairs to maintain regional stability and ensure debt repayment. Source: Wikipedia, Venezuelan Crisis of 1902–1903 — confirmed. Britannica, Roosevelt Corollary — confirmed.

SUPPORTED — THE DOCUMENTED PATTERN:

The U.S. government provided metal-backed coinage to Venezuela in 1876 as the inaugural act of its new commercial minting authority. Twenty-six years later the same government stood aside while European powers used a naval blockade to enforce debt collection against the same country. The coining relationship and the debt enforcement relationship operated through the same institutional framework — the Treasury and its commercial partners — without any documented acknowledgment of the connection between them. Whether any Treasury, State Department, or congressional record from 1902–1904 referenced the prior coining relationship with Venezuela in the context of the debt crisis requires review. The argument that these represent two faces of the same institutional dual standard is sound. The primary sources confirm both operations independently. The connection between them is SUPPORTED, not ESTABLISHED, pending review of the official record from that period.

7.7.2 China — The Mirror Image

ESTABLISHED — THE COINING RELATIONSHIP:

Approximately 1935, the San Francisco Federal Mint struck more than 10 million coins for the Chinese government over a two-month period. Employees worked behind bullet-proof enclosures during production. This is documented in National Archives holdings at the San Francisco facility and confirmed in National Archives Foundation records describing the Mint’s 1930s production for China during a silver and copper alloy shortage. Source: National Archives San Francisco — confirmed. National Archives Foundation — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — THE SIMULTANEOUS DOMESTIC DEPARTURE:

In 1935 the Chinese Nationalist government nationalized its major banks and in 1936 established a fiat currency system — transitioning away from the silver standard at the same moment the U.S. Mint was striking silver and copper coins for China. The U.S. domestic gold standard had been removed in 1933 under Executive Order 6102. In 1935 the U.S. Mint was providing metal-backed coins to a foreign government that was simultaneously transitioning to fiat — while the U.S. domestic population had already been moved off the gold standard two years earlier. Source: CEPR, Financial Revolution in Republican China 1900–1937 — confirmed. Executive Order 6102 — primary source confirmed.

SUPPORTED — THE MIRROR IMAGE:

The China 1935 example presents a mirror image of the domestic U.S. situation. The U.S. government removed its own citizens from the gold standard in 1933. Two years later it was providing metal-backed coins to a foreign government that was itself transitioning away from metal backing. Both governments moved their domestic populations toward fiat in the same era. The U.S. Mint served as a metal-backed coining contractor for both during the transition period. The institutional capacity for hard asset coinage was maintained commercially while being withdrawn from both domestic populations through policy. Whether any Treasury document from 1935 acknowledged this parallel is an open research question. The pattern is supported by the documented facts on both sides.

7.8.2 Argentina 1919–1920 — Treasury Document No. 2874 Confirmed

ESTABLISHED — SPECIFIC PRODUCTION:

Error coins from this period (Lincoln cents struck on Argentina 10-centavo planchets) document the presence of Argentine planchets in the Philadelphia Mint production environment. 9.3 UCC Article 9 — The Deposit Account Control Framework

ESTABLISHED — UCC ARTICLE 9 SCOPE AND AUTHORITY:

The Uniform Commercial Code is a model act jointly developed by the American Law Institute and the Uniform Law Commission and adopted in substantially similar form by all 50 U.S. states and the District of Columbia. Article 9 governs secured transactions — transactions in which a creditor obtains a security interest in a debtor’s personal property as collateral for an obligation. The statutory text cited in this section is taken from the official UCC text as published by the Legal Information Institute at Cornell Law School — law.cornell.edu/ucc — which is the standard public repository for the official UCC text. Source: UCC Article 9 official text — law.cornell.edu/ucc/9 — confirmed primary source.

ESTABLISHED — UCC 9-104: CONTROL OF DEPOSIT ACCOUNT (STATUTORY TEXT):

UCC Section 9-104 defines control of a deposit account. A secured party has control of a deposit account if: (1) the secured party is the bank with which the deposit account is maintained; (2) the debtor, secured party, and bank have agreed in an authenticated record that the bank will comply with instructions originated by the secured party directing disposition of the funds in the deposit account without further consent by the debtor; or (3) the secured party becomes the bank’s customer with respect to the deposit account. A secured party that has satisfied these conditions has control even if the debtor retains the right to direct the disposition of funds from the deposit account. Source: UCC 9-104, law.cornell.edu/ucc/9/9-104 — confirmed verbatim from primary statutory text.

ESTABLISHED — UCC 9-327: PRIORITY RULES (STATUTORY TEXT):

UCC Section 9-327 governs priority among conflicting security interests in the same deposit account. The confirmed statutory rules are: (1) A security interest held by a secured party having control of the deposit account under Section 9-104 has priority over a conflicting security interest held by a secured party that does not have control. (2) Security interests perfected by control rank according to priority in time of obtaining control. (3) A security interest held by the bank with which the deposit account is maintained has priority over a conflicting security interest held by another secured party. (4) A security interest perfected by control under Section 9-104(a)(3) has priority over a security interest held by the bank with which the deposit account is maintained. Source: UCC 9-327, law.cornell.edu/ucc/9/9-327 — confirmed verbatim from primary statutory text.

ESTABLISHED — THE OFFICIAL COMMENT ON PUBLIC NOTICE:

Official Comment 3 to UCC Section 9-104 states: No other form of public notice is necessary; all actual and potential creditors of the debtor are always on notice that the bank with which the debtor’s deposit account is maintained may assert a claim against the deposit account. This means that the bank’s priority interest in any deposit account it maintains requires no filing, no public record, and no separate agreement. The interest is automatic by operation of statute. Source: UCC 9-104 Official Comment 3, as documented in academic legal analysis of Article 9 deposit account provisions — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — PERFECTION BY CONTROL IS THE EXCLUSIVE METHOD:

Control is the exclusive mode of perfection for a security interest in a deposit account as original collateral under Article 9. Perfection by filing a financing statement is not available for deposit accounts as original collateral — only for deposit accounts claimed as proceeds of other Article 9 collateral. This means a depositor cannot file a UCC-1 financing statement to establish or assert a priority claim over their own deposit account as against the bank that maintains it. The bank’s control is automatic under 9-104(a)(1) by virtue of being the depository institution. Under 9-327(3), that automatic control confers statutory priority over any conflicting security interest held by any other party. Source: UCC 9-104, 9-314, 9-327 — confirmed from statutory text. Academic legal analysis of Article 9 deposit account provisions — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — CROSS-REFERENCE TO PART II:

The deposit created by that loan mechanism is then subject to the control framework documented in this section. Under UCC 9-104(a)(1), the bank that created the deposit by issuing the loan has automatic control of the deposit account in which those funds reside. The legal framework therefore documents the following sequence from named primary sources: the bank creates money as a deposit through lending (Bank of England confirmed), the bank simultaneously holds automatic statutory control over the deposit account containing that money (UCC 9-104 confirmed), and the bank holds statutory priority over any conflicting claim to that deposit (UCC 9-327 confirmed). 13.4 Bailey v. Alabama, 219 U.S. 219 (1911) — Confirmed Holding

ESTABLISHED — CASE FACTS:

Alonzo Bailey, a Black agricultural laborer in Alabama, signed a labor contract in December 1907 receiving a $15 advance against a $12 monthly salary. After approximately one month he quit and refused to refund the advance. Bailey was convicted and sentenced to a $30 fine and subsequently 136 days at hard labor for failing to pay the fine. He challenged the statute’s constitutionality. Source: Bailey v. Alabama, 219 U.S. 219 (1911) — Justia U.S. Supreme Court Center confirmed. Library of Congress U.S. Reports confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — HOLDING (7–2 MAJORITY, JUSTICE HUGHES):

The Supreme Court held the Alabama statute violated the Thirteenth Amendment. The Court confirmed the following legal standards that are entered here as ESTABLISHED from the primary source opinion text. First: The essence of peonage is compulsory service in payment of a debt. A peon is one who is compelled to work for his creditor until his debt is paid. Second: The contract exposes the debtor to liability for the loss due to the breach, but not to enforced labor. Third: What the state may not do directly it may not do indirectly. Fourth: The full intent of the constitutional provision could be defeated with obvious facility if through the guise of contracts under which advances had been made, debtors could be held to compulsory service. Fifth: The federal anti-peonage acts are necessarily violated by any state legislation which seeks to compel service or labor by making it a crime to fail or refuse to perform it. Source: Bailey v. Alabama, 219 U.S. 219, Justice Hughes majority opinion — confirmed verbatim from Justia primary source.

13.5 United States v. Reynolds, 235 U.S. 133 (1914) — Confirmed Holding

ESTABLISHED — CASE FACTS AND HOLDING:

Alabama law allowed private sureties to pay criminal fines on behalf of convicted persons, who then signed labor contracts to work off the debt to the surety. Ed Rivers was convicted of larceny, fined $58.75. Reynolds paid the fine and Rivers contracted to work for Reynolds for nearly ten months. When Rivers quit after one month, Reynolds had him arrested and convicted again, generating a new larger fine and a new longer contract. The Supreme Court held unanimously that this surety contract system constituted peonage and fell within the prohibition of the Thirteenth Amendment. The Court confirmed: Peonage is a condition of compulsory service based upon the indebtedness of the peon to the master. The basal fact is indebtedness. Constant fear of punishment under the criminal law renders work compulsory. The Court further confirmed that Congress undertook to strike down all laws, regulations, and usages in the states and territories which attempted to maintain and enforce, directly or indirectly, the voluntary or involuntary service or labor of any persons as peons in the liquidation of any debt or obligation. Source: United States v. Reynolds, 235 U.S. 133 (1914), Justice Day opinion — Justia, law.cornell.edu, and Library of Congress confirmed.

14.2 The Depositor-Creditor Relationship — Confirmed from Primary Source Cases

ESTABLISHED — CARR v. CARR (1811):

Carr v. Carr (1811), reported in Merivale 541n (1815–17), is the foundational English common law case establishing the legal nature of the bank deposit relationship. The question before the Master of the Rolls, Sir William Grant, was whether a cash balance due on a banker’s account constituted a debt that could pass by bequest. Sir William Grant held that it did, reasoning that deposited money could not have an earmark and that when money is paid into a banker’s account, the banker always opens a debtor and creditor account with the payor. This ruling established that the bank deposit creates a debtor-creditor relationship — the bank is the debtor, the depositor is the creditor — not a custodial or trust relationship. The deposited money becomes the bank’s property. The bank’s obligation is to repay an equivalent sum on demand. Source: Carr v. Carr (1811), Merivale 541n — confirmed from The Cobden Centre legal analysis citing primary source report — ESTABLISHED.

ESTABLISHED — FOLEY v. HILL (1848):

Foley v. Hill (1848) 2 HLC 28 (House of Lords) is the foundational English case that confirmed and extended the Carr v. Carr principle. Lord Chancellor Cottenham confirmed: Money, when paid into a bank, ceases altogether to be the money of the principal; it is by then the money of the banker, who is bound to return an equivalent by paying a similar sum to that deposited with him when he is asked for it. The case established that the banker-customer relationship is essentially a contract between a debtor and a creditor. The bank does not hold deposits in trust. It owns them and incurs a debt obligation to the depositor. This principle is confirmed as the cornerstone of banking law and has been consistently applied in U.S. banking law through the doctrine of general deposits. Source: Foley v. Hill (1848) 2 HLC 28 — confirmed from CliffsNotes legal analysis and Centaur University of Reading academic repository citing primary source judgment — ESTABLISHED.

ESTABLISHED — U.S. BANKING LAW CONFIRMATION:

The depositor-creditor, bank-debtor relationship is confirmed as established U.S. banking law. The bank is not a custodian of deposited funds. The deposited money becomes the property of the bank. The bank has borrowed the money but pays it back only when the depositor demands repayment. 15.2 The 1913 Delegation — What Congress Transferred

ESTABLISHED — THE FEDERAL RESERVE ACT 1913:

The Federal Reserve Act was enacted by Congress and signed into law by President Woodrow Wilson on December 23, 1913. It created the Federal Reserve System as the central bank of the United States and delegated to it the authority to issue Federal Reserve Notes — the paper currency of the United States. Prior to 1913, currency issuance was conducted under congressional supervision through the Treasury. The Federal Reserve Act transferred the operational function of money creation and currency issuance to the Federal Reserve System. This is confirmed in Part I of this document from the Federal Reserve Act primary source text and from the Congressional Research Service. Source: Federal Reserve Act 1913, Public Law 63-43 — confirmed in Part I Section 1.2. Congressional Research Service — confirmed Section 2.3.

ESTABLISHED — THE STRUCTURAL NATURE OF THE FEDERAL RESERVE:

The Federal Reserve System has a dual structure confirmed from primary sources. The Board of Governors is a federal agency whose seven members are nominated by the President and confirmed by the Senate. The twelve regional Federal Reserve Banks are organized as corporations whose stockholders are the commercial banks in each Federal Reserve district. The Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals addressed this dual structure in Lewis v. United States, 680 F.2d 1239 (9th Cir. 1982). The court confirmed that Federal Reserve Banks are not federal instrumentalities for purposes of the Federal Tort Claims Act but are independent privately owned and locally controlled corporations. 15.3 The IRS Authority Chain — Delegation Upon Delegation

15.4 The Core Constitutional Question — Can Congress Delegate the Power of the Purse?

ESTABLISHED — THE NON-DELEGATION DOCTRINE:

The non-delegation doctrine is the constitutional principle that Congress cannot delegate its legislative powers to another branch or to a private entity. It derives from Article I Section 1 of the Constitution, which vests all legislative power in Congress. The Supreme Court has applied this doctrine throughout U.S. constitutional history. In its strongest applications, the Court has required that when Congress delegates authority it must provide an intelligible principle guiding the delegate’s discretion. The doctrine has been invoked but rarely used to strike down legislation in the modern era. The Supreme Court struck down two New Deal statutes on non-delegation grounds in A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States, 295 U.S. 495 (1935) and Panama Refining Co. v. Ryan, 293 U.S. 388 (1935). These remain the last instances in which the Supreme Court struck down legislation on non-delegation grounds. Source: A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States 295 U.S. 495 (1935) — confirmed. PART XVI: THE 1951 ACCORD, FEDERAL RESERVE SELF-FUNDING, AND THE INTRAGOVERNMENTAL DEBT STRUCTURE

16.1 The Treasury-Federal Reserve Accord of 1951 — Confirmed Primary Source

ESTABLISHED — THE ACCORD TEXT:

On March 4, 1951, the U.S. Treasury Department and the Federal Reserve System issued a joint public statement. The confirmed text of that statement, reproduced verbatim from the Federal Reserve Board of Governors 38th Annual Report and confirmed from the FRASER archive of the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis, reads: The Treasury and the Federal Reserve System have reached full accord with respect to debt management and monetary policies to be pursued in furthering their common purpose to assure the successful financing of the Government’s requirements and, at the same time, to minimize monetization of the public debt. Source: Federal Reserve Board of Governors, 38th Annual Report, FOMC minutes March 1951 — confirmed from federalreserve.gov. FRASER archive, Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis — fraser.stlouisfed.org confirmed. Federal Reserve History essay, federalreservehistory.org — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — WHAT THE ACCORD DID:

The Federal Reserve History essay published at federalreservehistory.org describes the Accord as effecting the liberation of monetary policy and laying the foundation for the modern Federal Reserve. Prior to the Accord, the Federal Reserve had committed during World War II at the request of the Treasury to maintaining an interest rate peg of 3/8 percent on short-term Treasury bills, with a 2.5 percent ceiling on long-term bonds. This peg subordinated Federal Reserve monetary policy to Treasury debt management requirements. The Accord separated these two functions. From March 1951 forward the Federal Reserve operated its monetary policy independently of Treasury debt management obligations. The Accord did not transfer any congressional authority. It was an executive branch agreement between two institutions — the Treasury Department and the Federal Reserve System — neither of which holds Article I monetary power under the constitutional analysis documented in Part XV. Source: Federal Reserve History essay, federalreservehistory.org — confirmed. FRASER timeline confirmed. Federal Reserve Bank of Richmond Economic Quarterly 87 no. 1 Winter 2001 — confirmed secondary source.

ESTABLISHED — WHAT THE ACCORD DID NOT PUBLISH:

Academic research by Barry Eichengreen and Peter M. Garber, published by the National Bureau of Economic Research, confirms that the exact provisions of the agreement between the Federal Reserve Board and the Treasury were never published. The public statement documented above was the entirety of the officially published record. The operational details of the Accord — specifically the commitment to support the price of five-year notes for a short period before the bond market would be left on its own — were negotiated but not publicly disclosed at the time. This is entered as ESTABLISHED because it is confirmed from named academic primary sources and is relevant to the document’s broader analysis of what is and is not in the public record. 16.2 The Federal Reserve Self-Funding Mechanism — Confirmed Structure

ESTABLISHED — HOW THE FEDERAL RESERVE FUNDS ITSELF:

The Federal Reserve does not receive congressional appropriations. It funds its operations primarily from interest earned on the securities it holds — predominantly U.S. Treasury securities acquired through open market operations. Over 90% of Federal Reserve revenues come from open market operations, specifically interest on the portfolio of Treasury securities and capital gains or losses from buying and selling those securities. The balance comes from fee-based income for financial services and discount window loans. The Federal Reserve Board requires by law that excess earnings of the Reserve Banks be transferred to the U.S. Treasury as interest on Federal Reserve notes. This mechanism means that in normal operating periods the Federal Reserve collects interest on Treasury securities it purchased with created reserves, deducts its operating expenses, and remits the remainder to the Treasury. Source: Federal Reserve Wikipedia article citing Federal Reserve Board publications — confirmed. Federal Reserve Board of Governors Annual Report — confirmed institutional publication. Federal Reserve Board — federalreserve.gov — confirmed.

ESTABLISHED — THE DEFERRED ASSET: REMITTANCES SUSPENDED SINCE 2022:

The Federal Reserve’s net income turned negative in late 2022 for the first time in decades. When net income is negative the Federal Reserve does not reduce capital, become insolvent, or require a government capital infusion. Instead it creates a deferred asset — a negative liability on its balance sheet — representing the cumulative shortfall in earnings. Remittances to the Treasury are suspended until the deferred asset is paid down to zero from future positive net income. The Congressional Research Service report Federal Reserve: Policy Issues in the 119th Congress, published by Congress.gov, confirms that at the end of the third quarter of 2025 the deferred asset was $243 billion. The Congressional Budget Office projected in May 2025 that remittances to Treasury would not resume until FY2030. The Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis confirmed that as of November 2023 the deferred asset had reached $116.9 billion. The growth from $116.9 billion to $243 billion between November 2023 and September 2025 represents continued accumulation. This means the Federal Reserve has made no net remittances to the Treasury since 2022 and will not do so until at least FY2030 under current CBO projections. Source: Congressional Research Service, Federal Reserve: Policy Issues in the 119th Congress — congress.gov confirmed. Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis, On the Economy blog, November 2023 — confirmed. CBO projection May 2025 — confirmed from CRS report.

ESTABLISHED — CUMULATIVE REMITTANCES SINCE 2010:

This figure covers the period of large-scale asset purchases following the 2008 financial crisis and the COVID-19 pandemic response, during which the Federal Reserve’s balance sheet expanded significantly, generating substantial interest income before the 2022 rate increases made net income negative. ESTABLISHED — CASE FACTS AND HOLDING:
