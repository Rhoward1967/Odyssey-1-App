- Article 18 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights

### Injury Two: Economic Exploitation and Wealth Extraction

The people collectively constitute the fundamental bond of the national economy — the value of fiat currency, the creditworthiness of the government, and overall economic stability are demonstrably dependent upon the ongoing labor, consumption, innovation, and trust of the populace — yet the populace receives no direct financial compensation or interest for this foundational contribution.

For the descendants of enslaved Africans specifically, this extraction was not abstract. It was chattel slavery, convict leasing, redlining, predatory lending, and the systematic exclusion from wealth-building mechanisms available to others. The 13th Amendment's punishment clause preserved forced labor. Under Jones v. Alfred H. Mayer Co., 392 U.S. 409 (1968), Congress holds the power to address the lingering badges and incidents of slavery. Under Clyatt v. United States, 197 U.S. 207 (1905), peonage — compelled service for debt — was declared unconstitutional. The economic structure imposed upon my ancestral community meets the historical and legal definition of systemic extraction, not consensual participation.

### Injury Three: Physical Liberty and Unlawful Detention

Civil rights violations under color of law — 18 U.S.C. §§ 241 and 242 — constitute federal crimes for any person acting under law to willfully deprive constitutional or federally protected rights. Any detention or restriction of my physical liberty that relies on racially disparate enforcement constitutes a violation of the Fourth, Fifth, and Fourteenth Amendments, and Article 9 of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights.
