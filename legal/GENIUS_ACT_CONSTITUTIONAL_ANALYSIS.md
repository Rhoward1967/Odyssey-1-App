## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The GENIUS Act (Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for U.S. Stablecoins Act) contains not one but multiple layers of constitutional violation arising from a single foundational defect: it purports to create a new monetary system while funding that system entirely with the debt instruments of an admitted insolvent sovereign. Every violation downstream of that original defect inherits its constitutional nullity.

This analysis documents six independent grounds on which the GENIUS Act is constitutionally void from inception — not defective, not improvable, but void — because it operates from a premise that law cannot recognize: that a bankrupt sovereign's promises constitute lawful money.

## PART I — THE FOUNDATIONAL DEFECT: FUNDED BY INSOLVENCY

### 1.1 — What the Act Requires

The GENIUS Act mandates that "payment stablecoin" issuers back their digital assets 1:1 with "permitted assets." The primary permitted assets are:

- U.S. Treasury bills (T-bills)
- U.S. Treasury notes and bonds
- Federal Reserve deposits
- Repurchase agreements collateralized by the above

Every item on this list is an instrument of U.S. sovereign debt.

### 1.2 — The Admitted Condition of the Issuing Sovereign

The United States government, the issuer of the "permitted assets," has publicly documented its own financial condition:

| Metric | Documented Figure | Source |
|--------|-----------------|--------|
| Total Federal Assets | $6.06 trillion | Treasury FY2025 Consolidated Financial Statements |
| Total Federal Liabilities | $47.78 trillion | Treasury FY2025 Consolidated Financial Statements |
| **Net Balance Sheet Position** | **NEGATIVE $41.72 trillion** | Treasury FY2025 Consolidated Financial Statements |
| Off-balance-sheet obligations | $136.2 trillion+ | Treasury FY2025 Consolidated Financial Statements |
| GAO Audit Qualification | **Disclaimer of Opinion** | GAO — 29 consecutive years, FY1996–FY2024 |

A Disclaimer of Opinion is the most severe audit finding possible. It means the government's own auditors cannot determine whether the financial statements are fairly presented. The "permitted assets" mandated by the GENIUS Act are instruments issued by a sovereign whose own auditors cannot certify its financial condition.

### 1.3 — The Mathematical Reality

The circular debt loop operates as follows:

```
Private entity issues stablecoin → must hold Treasury debt as "reserve"
       ↓
Treasury issues debt → backed by future tax revenue of an insolvent government
       ↓
Future tax revenue → collected in Federal Reserve Notes
       ↓
Federal Reserve Notes → backed by... more Treasury debt
       ↓
RESULT: Stablecoin "backed" by debt backed by debt backed by debt
        with no gold, silver, or productive asset anywhere in the chain
```

This is not a monetary system. This is a closed loop of obligation with no asset at the center.

## PART II — VIOLATION 1: ARTICLE I, SECTION 10 (LEGAL TENDER CLAUSE)

### 2.1 — The Constitutional Text

> *"No State shall... make any Thing but gold and silver Coin a Tender in Payment of Debts."*
> — U.S. Constitution, Article I, Section 10

### 2.2 — How the GENIUS Act Violates This

The GENIUS Act authorizes state-chartered entities to issue "payment stablecoins" — digital instruments explicitly designed for the settlement and payment of debts. By permitting states to charter stablecoin issuers whose instruments function as payment tender, the Act creates a state-sanctioned tender that is not gold or silver coin.

The constitutional text is not ambiguous. States may not make anything but gold and silver coin a tender in payment of debts. A state-chartered digital instrument backed by Treasury debt is neither gold nor silver. It is constitutionally void as legal tender from the moment of authorization.

### 2.3 — The HJR-192 Corollary

Congress acknowledged in House Joint Resolution 192 (June 5, 1933) that gold clause obligations could be discharged in Federal Reserve Notes. The corollary — never adjudicated but legally unavoidable — is that where the Federal Reserve Note itself is the product of an insolvent sovereign, the instrument of discharge is constitutionally defective. The GENIUS Act extends this defect forward into digital form.

## PART III — VIOLATION 2: FIFTH AMENDMENT (TAKING WITHOUT COMPENSATION)

### 3.1 — The Mechanism

When a citizen is required to conduct commerce in an instrument whose reserve backing is the debt of an admitted insolvent sovereign — without disclosure of that insolvency — the citizen's productive labor and property are exchanged for instruments that cannot hold equivalent value. This constitutes a taking of private property without just compensation in violation of the Fifth Amendment.

### 3.2 — The Disclosure Failure

The GENIUS Act mandates monthly "attestations" by private issuers regarding their reserves. It does not require disclosure to stablecoin holders that:

- The U.S. Treasury has a negative $41.72 trillion net position
- The GAO has been unable to certify federal financial statements for 29 consecutive years
- The "reserves" are instruments of an entity that has admitted it cannot account for its own finances

A contract entered without disclosure of material facts known to one party constitutes fraud in the inducement. A statutory framework that mandates such non-disclosure at scale constitutes a systematic taking.

## PART IV — VIOLATION 3: DUE PROCESS (FIFTH AND FOURTEENTH AMENDMENTS)

### 4.1 — Section 11: The Insolvency Trap

Section 11 of the GENIUS Act addresses issuer insolvency and claims to give stablecoin holders "first priority" over reserves in a bankruptcy proceeding.

### 4.2 — The Hidden Subordination

Legal analysis of Section 11 reveals that in an actual liquidation, the priority order operates as follows:

1. Bankruptcy professionals (attorneys, trustees, administrators)
2. Repo lenders with custody of Treasury collateral
3. Margin lenders with perfected security interests
4. Federal regulators with statutory priority claims
5. **Stablecoin holders** — last among secured claimants

The Act tells the public they have "first priority." The operational reality places them fifth. This misrepresentation of legal rights — codified in statute — denies due process by concealing the true risk profile of the instrument from those who must use it for commerce.

### 4.3 — Administrative Insolvency

If the Treasury instruments held as reserves decline in value — which they must if the sovereign's $41.72 trillion negative position deteriorates further — the issuer becomes "administratively insolvent." In administrative insolvency, the cost of the bankruptcy proceeding itself consumes the remaining reserve value before holders receive any distribution. Section 11 provides no protection against this scenario.

## PART VI — VIOLATION 5: ARTICLE I, SECTION 8 (COINAGE CLAUSE)

### 6.1 — The Constitutional Delegation

Article I, Section 8 grants Congress the power "To coin Money, regulate the Value thereof, and of foreign Coin." This power is delegated exclusively to the federal government. The founding generation understood "coining money" to mean the production of intrinsically valuable metallic currency — not the authorization of private entities to issue digital obligations backed by debt.

### 6.2 — Improper Delegation to Private Issuers

The GENIUS Act delegates the functional power to create money — the issuance of instruments designed for universal debt settlement — to private corporations, state-chartered entities, and non-bank financial institutions. This delegation of a constitutionally reserved federal power to private entities is constitutionally impermissible.

The Federal Reserve Act of 1913 established the first layer of this delegation. The GENIUS Act extends it further into the private sector. Each layer of delegation moves further from the constitutional grant and deeper into constitutional nullity.
