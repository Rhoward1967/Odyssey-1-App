**Response:** "The UCC-1 makes the Trust's ownership interest visible in the public record, preventing future creditors from claiming priority. It's a standard secured creditor practice when a business has significant IP assets. The filing protects the Trust's $6.71 billion IP portfolio from future judgment creditors."

**Objection 3:** "We need to see the notarized documents before we open the accounts."

**Response:** "Understood. We'll have the documents notarized this week and can bring originals back to you. In the meantime, can we open the accounts with our current signatures, and you can receive the notarized pages when they're ready? Or would you prefer to wait until we have the notarized documents?"

**Objection 4:** "The Grantor should probably be a signatory on the accounts."

**Response:** "The Grantor's exclusion from signature authority is intentional and required by our asset protection strategy. He has established an irrevocable trust precisely to separate himself from asset control. If creditors can prove he controls the assets, the asset protection fails. The Co-Trustees have full operational authority, so there's no operational constraint."

**Objection 5:** "This structure seems overly complex for a startup."
