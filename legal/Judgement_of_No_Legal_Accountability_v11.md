PART II: INSTITUTIONAL CONFLICT OF INTEREST

2.1 The Courts' Own Stated Principles Applied To The Institution

Justice requires impartiality. Impartiality requires the absence of conflict of interest. Courts have a material financial stake in the monetary system they adjudicate. Therefore courts cannot be genuinely impartial in cases involving that system. This logical chain applies the courts' own stated standards consistently — the same standards they apply to individual judges — to the institution collectively.



SUPPORTED: Courts apply conflict of interest standards strictly to individual participants in proceedings. There is no equivalent mechanism requiring institutional recusal when the court as an institution has a systemic financial interest in the outcome. The nemo iudex in causa sua principle — no person shall be a judge in their own cause — is a foundational principle of natural justice recognized in English common law and taught in every law school. It is not applied at the institutional level.



2.2 The Precedent Problem

SUPPORTED: If the original opinion in a line of cases was rendered by a court with a structural conflict of interest then every court following that precedent compounds the original bias. ESTABLISHED: Historical examples demonstrate that precedent can institutionalize bias for decades. Plessy v. Ferguson stood for 58 years. Dred Scott stood until the Civil War. Korematsu was not formally repudiated until 2018. These are documented in court records.

ESTABLISHED: A formal bill introduced in the United States Congress — House Resolution 2990 of the 112th Congress — contained this language in its findings: In 1913 Congress effectively delegated the sovereign power to create money to the Federal Reserve system and private financial industry. ESTABLISHED: The constitutional question of whether Congress could delegate its monetary authority to a private banking system was raised at the time of passage and has never been definitively resolved by the Supreme Court. Legal scholarship from Cornell University confirms this.

PART VII: NATURAL LAW VS. MAN'S LAW — THE CORE TENSION

7.1 The Honest Framing

At the heart of this research sits a genuine tension between two legal frameworks that have been in conflict since the founding of the republic. Natural law holds that law derives legitimacy from conforming to universal moral principles and that unjust law carries no moral authority regardless of how it was enacted. Legal positivism holds that law is what the recognized authority declares it to be through proper process and that legitimacy comes from enactment not from moral content.



SUPPORTED: The founders explicitly used natural law arguments to justify the Revolution — citing Locke's framework that when government violates natural rights revolution becomes a right and a duty. Having used natural law to establish their authority they then built a system based on positivism — applying man's law strictly to everyone subject to it. That is a documented inconsistency in the founding framework itself.



ESTABLISHED: Courts operating within the positivist framework do not recognize natural law as overriding enacted law. Arguments based on natural law or the illegitimacy of the monetary system have been consistently rejected as frivolous in current courts. This is documented in court records. This does not mean natural law arguments are wrong. It means they have no current practical legal force within the existing system. Those are two separate questions this research keeps distinct.
