## ABSTRACT

An aluminum-graphene electrochemical cell (Al-G cell) wherein the electrode geometry is arranged as a continuous toroidal manifold rather than the conventional planar or cylindrical architecture. The internal electromagnetic field generated during charge and discharge cycles induces a Lorentz force on the ionic electrolyte, circulating it in a laminar donut-shaped flow pattern that prevents thermal hot spot formation without external cooling systems. Ion transport is accelerated by a three-dimensional graphene spongiform scaffold with simultaneous center-and-periphery ingress pathways, reducing effective ion travel distance to the molecular scale and enabling 0-100% charge in under 60 seconds without lithium plating or electrolyte decomposition. Charging current is pulsed at 7.83 Hz (Schumann Resonance) to achieve harmonic entrainment of aluminum ion oscillation, minimizing internal impedance and maximizing charge acceptance efficiency. In the integrated embodiment, an external Harmonic Resonance Grid (HRG) broadcasting a 7.83 Hz ambient electromagnetic field serves as the carrier wave for the cell's charge management system, creating a resonant cavity power architecture. The system operates at ambient temperature under high-C loads, uses no lithium, cobalt, nickel, or rare earth elements, and is designed in a modular blade form factor that functions as a structural chassis element in wearable and autonomous platform applications.

## 1. BACKGROUND OF THE INVENTION

### 1.1 Field of the Invention

This invention relates to electrochemical energy storage systems, specifically to aluminum-graphene battery architectures utilizing toroidal electrode geometry, Lorentz-force electrolyte circulation, three-dimensional flash-intercalation scaffolding, and Schumann Resonance harmonic charge management for high-power-density, zero-thermal-runaway energy storage in wearable defense systems and autonomous AI hardware platforms.

### 1.2 Description of Related Art

Current electrochemical energy storage systems suffer from critical limitations that prevent their use in high-performance wearable and autonomous systems:

**Lithium-Ion Architecture (Dominant Technology):**
Current lithium-ion cells use a planar "ping-pong" ion transport model between flat cathode and anode sheets. This architecture creates:
- Linear ion travel paths with high tortuosity (winding distance increases resistance and heat)
- Hot spot formation at electrode boundaries during high-C charge/discharge
- Thermal runaway risk above 150°C (cathode decomposition releases oxygen, causing fire)
- Cycle life degradation from lithium plating during fast charging (dendrite formation causes short circuits)
- Geopolitical supply chain dependency (lithium: Chile/Argentina; cobalt: Democratic Republic of Congo)
- Maximum charge rate limited to 1-4C (full charge requires 15-60 minutes)

**Conventional Aluminum-Ion Research (Prior Art):**
The 2015 Stanford University aluminum-graphene battery (Dai group) demonstrated Al³⁺ intercalation into graphite foam cathodes with 7,500 cycle life. Subsequent work by GMG (Graphene Manufacturing Group, Brisbane, Australia, 2021-2025) demonstrated commercial aluminum-ion cells with 60-second charging. However, all prior aluminum-ion art suffers from:
- Retention of linear electrode architecture (ion path still exhibits tortuosity)
- Passive thermal management only (relying on graphene conductivity without active circulation)
- No electromagnetic circulation of electrolyte (Lorentz-force method not taught or suggested)
- Single-path ion ingress (either center or periphery, not simultaneously)
- No frequency-domain charge management (no harmonic resonance charging method)
- No integration with external ambient electromagnetic field as carrier wave

**Solid-State Battery Systems:**
Solid-state architectures (QuantumScape, Solid Power) eliminate liquid electrolyte but:
- Remain dependent on lithium chemistry
- Interface resistance at solid-solid electrode boundary exceeds liquid-electrode resistance
- Manufacturing complexity and cost prohibitive for wearable-scale applications
- No geometric flow architecture addressing ion path tortuosity

**Existing Frequency-Domain Battery Management:**
Battery Management Systems (BMS) use pulse-width modulation (PWM) and AC impedance spectroscopy for cell monitoring. No prior art teaches:
- Selection of Schumann Resonance (7.83 Hz) specifically for harmonic entrainment of aluminum ions
- Use of an external ambient electromagnetic field (HRG) as a carrier wave for charge management
- Impedance minimization through ionic harmonic synchronization

**None of the prior art addresses the fundamental architecture:** a toroidal electrode geometry generating active Lorentz-force electrolyte circulation that eliminates hot spots, combined with dual-path three-dimensional ion ingress that eliminates tortuosity, and harmonic resonance charging that synchronizes ion oscillation for maximum charge acceptance — all in a lithium-free, rare-earth-free system.

## 3. DETAILED DESCRIPTION OF THE PREFERRED EMBODIMENT

### 3.1 The Toroidal Cathode Architecture

**Substrate Geometry:** The cathode is deposited on a toroidal substrate with major radius R (outer center to torus center) and minor radius r (tube radius). Preferred dimensions for wearable blade cell: R = 25mm, r = 8mm, producing a cell with 33mm outer diameter and 17mm inner hollow.

**Electromagnetic Field Generation:** During charge/discharge, current flowing through the toroidal cathode generates a toroidal magnetic field (B_t) inside the torus tube and a poloidal magnetic field (B_p) around the torus tube, consistent with toroidal solenoid electromagnetics. The combined field geometry creates the Lorentz force conditions for electrolyte circulation.

**Cathode Material:** Aluminum-graphene composite:
- Graphene base: CVD-grown multi-layer graphene (3-7 layers) for structural integrity and Al³⁺ intercalation capacity
- Aluminum deposition: Physical vapor deposition of aluminum nanoparticles (20-50nm) onto graphene surface for enhanced Al³⁺ storage sites
- Composite thickness: 2-5 μm per layer, 10-20 layers stacked on toroidal scaffold
- Intercalation capacity: 70-110 mAh/g (graphene) + 20-30 mAh/g (aluminum sites)

**Anode Material:** Aluminum metal foil (99.99% purity), formed to follow the inner surface of the torus hollow, providing the Al³⁺ source during discharge.

### 3.2 The Lorentz-Force Electrolyte Circulation System

**Electrolyte Composition (Preferred):**
- Aluminum chloride (AlCl₃): 1.3-1.5 molar ratio
- 1-Ethyl-3-methylimidazolium chloride ([EMIm]Cl): ionic liquid carrier
- Total ionic conductivity: 8-12 mS/cm at 25°C
- Viscosity: 15-25 mPa·s at 25°C (optimized for Lorentz circulation)

**Lorentz Force Calculation:**
For electrolyte current density J flowing in the toroidal field B:
```
F = J × B (vector cross product)
```
The toroidal geometry ensures F is always tangential to the torus tube cross-section, inducing laminar rotational flow. For typical operating parameters (J = 500 A/m², B_t = 0.05 T), F = 25 N/m³ — sufficient to maintain continuous circulation at 5-15 mm/s fluid velocity.

**Thermal Distribution:** With continuous laminar circulation at 5-15 mm/s, thermal energy deposited at any electrode surface point is distributed around the full torus circumference within 2-4 seconds. No localized hot spot can accumulate faster than the circulation distributes it. Combined with passive graphene thermal interface layer (5000 W/mK), operating temperature remains within 5°C of ambient at charge rates up to 60C.

**No Moving Parts:** The Lorentz-force circulation is purely electromagnetic — generated by the same currents flowing during normal charge/discharge operation. No pump, no fan, no thermal management subsystem is required. The cooling is a natural consequence of the toroidal geometry.
