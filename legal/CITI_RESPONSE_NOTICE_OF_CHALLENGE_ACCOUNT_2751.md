## PART II — YOUR DEBT VALIDATION RESPONSE IS LEGALLY INSUFFICIENT

Your letter of March 19, 2026 claims the debt is valid based upon:
- Monthly statements sent to the address of record
- The last monthly statement
- The date the account was opened (2/29/2024)
- A claim of Truth in Lending Act compliance
- A claim that as a national bank you are not required to hold a debt collection license

**This response fails to constitute adequate debt validation for the following reasons:**

### 2.1 — Missing Original Signed Agreement
You have not provided the original wet-ink signed credit agreement bearing the actual signature of Rickey Allan Howard. Monthly statements are your own records — they are not proof of a valid original obligation. Under 15 USC §1692g, proper validation requires more than a creditor's own internal account records.

### 2.2 — Missing Documentation of Credit Limit Increase
Your own application records confirm the original approved credit limit was **$3,000.00**. The credit limit was subsequently increased to **$6,700.00** — a 123% increase. You have provided no documentation showing:
- When this increase was made
- What agreement authorized this increase
- Whether Rickey Allan Howard provided written consent to this increase
- What consideration was exchanged for the new terms

Any balance above the original $3,000 approved limit is subject to challenge as unauthorized under the original agreement.

### 2.3 — Missing Proof of Lawful Consideration
You have not demonstrated that Citibank, N.A. extended lawful consideration at the time of contracting. As a fractional reserve national bank, Citibank creates credit entries from reserve multipliers — not from pre-existing deposits of lawful money. You must demonstrate:
- The source of the actual funds extended
- That the consideration was lawful money as defined at law
- That you suffered actual loss equal to the amount claimed

### 2.4 — Missing Chain of Title and Holder Status
You must demonstrate that Citibank, N.A. is the current holder in due course of this instrument and has not securitized, bundled, sold, or assigned any interest in this account to any third party. Under UCC 3-301 and UCC 3-302, only a holder in due course may enforce a negotiable instrument.

## PART IV — FAILURE OF LAWFUL CONSIDERATION — THE INSOLVENCY DOCTRINE

This is the foundational challenge. The following facts are drawn exclusively from official United States government publications and are matters of public record.

### 4.1 — The Treasury's Admitted Insolvency
The United States Treasury Department's own Consolidated Financial Statements for Fiscal Year 2025 disclose:
- **Total Federal Assets: $6.06 trillion**
- **Total Federal Liabilities: $47.78 trillion**
- **Net Balance Sheet Position: NEGATIVE $41.72 trillion**
- Total liabilities are nearly **eight times** the value of reported assets
- The balance sheet deteriorated by $2.07 trillion in a single fiscal year
- Total obligations including off-balance-sheet items exceed **$136.2 trillion**

### 4.2 — Twenty-Nine Consecutive Years of Unauditable Financials
The Government Accountability Office has issued a **Disclaimer of Opinion** on the United States government's financial statements for **twenty-nine consecutive years**. This means the government's own auditors cannot confirm whether the financial statements are fairly presented. This is not an opinion — it is the documented official position of the United States Government Accountability Office.

### 4.3 — Former U.S. Comptroller General Confirmation
Former United States Comptroller General David M. Walker — the government's own former chief auditor — confirmed publicly on March 23, 2026 that the conclusion of insolvency is drawn directly from the Treasury Department's own published statements. This constitutes an admission by the highest former financial officer of the United States.

### 4.4 — The Legal Consequence for This Account
Every obligation denominated in United States Federal Reserve Notes — including Account 2751 — is an instrument whose underlying consideration is currency issued by a documented insolvent sovereign.

Under **O.C.G.A. § 13-3-40**, consideration is essential to a valid contract. Where the sovereign issuing the currency has admitted that its liabilities exceed its assets by $41.72 trillion, the currency denominating the obligation cannot constitute valid consideration in law.

Under **O.C.G.A. § 13-5-5**, fraud renders contracts voidable at the election of the injured party. The concealment of the sovereign's financial condition for twenty-nine consecutive years while inducing citizens to enter financial obligations denominated in that sovereign's currency constitutes fraud in the inducement.

Under **O.C.G.A. § 23-2-51**, nondisclosure of material facts by a party with an obligation to communicate them constitutes constructive fraud. The government had an obligation to disclose its financial condition and failed to do so for twenty-nine consecutive years.

### 4.5 — Constitutional Foundation
The Constitution of the United States, **Article I, Section 10**, provides that no state shall make anything but gold and silver coin a tender in payment of debts. The abandonment of gold convertibility severed the dollar from its constitutional monetary foundation. Federal Reserve Notes are instruments of debt — not lawful money as defined by the Constitution. An obligation denominated exclusively in an unconstitutional monetary instrument carries a constitutional defect that runs with the instrument.

**Under HJR-192 (June 5, 1933)**, Congress acknowledged that gold clause obligations could be discharged in Federal Reserve Notes. The corollary is that where the Federal Reserve Note itself is the product of an insolvent sovereign, the instrument of discharge is itself constitutionally defective.

### 4.6 — Citibank's Own Position as a Fractional Reserve Institution
As a national bank, Citibank, N.A. extends credit through fractional reserve multiplication — not from pre-existing reserves of lawful money. The "consideration" Citibank extended was a bookkeeping entry created at the moment of account activation, not a transfer of pre-existing wealth. This Court, in *First National Bank of Montgomery v. Jerome Daly* (1968), and related authorities have established that the extension of credit created from nothing does not constitute adequate consideration for a binding obligation. Citibank must demonstrate it suffered actual loss equal to the amount it now claims.
