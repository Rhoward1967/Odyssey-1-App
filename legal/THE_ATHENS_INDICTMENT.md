## PREAMBLE: WHY THIS DOCUMENT IS NECESSARY

The Founding Crime Treatise established the national case. But the national enterprise operates on specific ground. It commits specific crimes against specific people in specific places. Athens, Georgia is one of those places. The Muscogee Creek who were dispossessed of this land. The enslaved persons who built its economy. The Black citizens who were terrorized, convict-leased, redlined, and systematically excluded from the wealth generated on the land their ancestors worked. The indigenous peoples whose cultural memory was erased from the landscape that carries it.

The University of Georgia sits on that land today. The Clarke County legal system operates on those precedents today. The institutions that benefited from the specific crimes committed on this specific ground continue to operate, to grow, to generate wealth, and to issue apologies that change nothing about who holds the land, who controls the institutions, and who continues to experience the enterprise's enforcement.

An apology without restitution is a performance. A land acknowledgment without land return is a ceremony. A diversity statement without structural change is a document.

## PART ONE: THE GROUND — Muscogee Creek Territory and the Doctrine of Discovery

The land on which Athens, Georgia stands was Muscogee Creek territory for thousands of years before European contact. The Muscogee had towns, trade networks, ceremonial practices, agricultural systems, and legal traditions that predated by millennia the colonial legal fiction that would be used to dispossess them.

The legal instrument of dispossession was the Doctrine of Discovery — originating in papal bulls of the 15th century and adopted into American law by the Supreme Court in *Johnson v. M'Intosh*, 21 U.S. 543 (1823) — that Christian European nations could claim sovereignty over lands inhabited by non-Christian peoples simply by arriving, planting a flag, and declaring discovery.

**EVIDENCE:** *Johnson v. M'Intosh* — Chief Justice John Marshall wrote that the principle of discovery gave European nations the exclusive right to extinguish the Indian title of occupancy. The native inhabitants were admitted to be the rightful occupants of the soil but their rights to complete sovereignty were necessarily diminished by the original fundamental principle.

**EVIDENCE:** Treaty of Fort Jackson, August 9, 1814 — Following the Creek War, Andrew Jackson forced the Creek Nation to cede approximately 23 million acres under military duress.

**EVIDENCE:** Indian Removal Act, 4 Stat. 411 (1830) — Authorized the forced removal of the Creek, Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, and Seminole nations. Approximately 3,500 Creek people died during the removal. The land became available for the establishment of institutions including the University of Georgia.

**PRESENT TENSE:** The University of Georgia occupies that land today. Its endowment grows on it. The Muscogee Creek receive no benefit. The institution has issued a land acknowledgment statement. It has not returned the land. It said it was sorry. Then it continued.

**FINDING:** The ground on which Athens stands is Muscogee Creek territory obtained through a colonial legal doctrine characterized as a violation of fundamental human rights principles. The institutions built on that ground continue to benefit from the dispossession. The crime is ongoing in the form of continued possession without restitution.

## PART TWO: THE UNIVERSITY — How UGA Was Built on Stolen Land and Enslaved Labor

The University of Georgia is the oldest public university chartered by a state government — chartered in 1785, built on stolen land by enslaved labor, and operated for most of its history as an institution that explicitly excluded the descendants of the people who built it.

**EVIDENCE:** Georgia Legislature, Land Grant Charter of UGA, 1785 — The act chartered 40,000 acres drawn from territory obtained through the progressive extinguishment of Creek title through treaty and military action.

**EVIDENCE:** UGA's Report on the History of Slavery at the University of Georgia (2020) — The University's own commissioned report documented that enslaved persons were held by University presidents, faculty, and trustees; that enslaved labor was used in construction and maintenance; and that the University's early operations were intertwined with the slave economy of Georgia. The University acknowledged these facts. It has not provided reparative measures proportionate to the benefit received.

**EVIDENCE:** *Holmes v. Danner*, 191 F. Supp. 394 (M.D. Ga. 1961) — Federal District Judge William Bootle ordered the University to admit Charlayne Hunter and Hamilton Holmes — the first Black students. On January 11, 1961, white students rioted. University officials suspended Hunter and Holmes for their own safety — punishing the victims of the riot. Judge Bootle ordered them reinstated. Federal force was required for the University to honor the constitutional guarantee of equal protection that had existed since 1868.

**PRESENT TENSE:** The University of Georgia today holds an endowment exceeding one billion dollars. It has issued reports, acknowledgments, and diversity statements. It has not provided land restitution. It has not provided reparative compensation to the descendants of enslaved persons who built its infrastructure.

**FINDING:** The University of Georgia is a direct and continuing institutional beneficiary of the Creek dispossession and the enslaved labor system. Its acknowledgments have not been accompanied by remedial measures proportionate to the benefit received or the harm caused.

176 years of exclusion. A federal court order to integrate. A riot against the students the order protected. A diversity statement. The endowment grows. The chain is lighter. It is still a chain.

## PART THREE: THE CONVICT LEASING ERA IN CLARKE COUNTY

Following the formal abolition of slavery in 1865, the State of Georgia and Clarke County implemented the convict leasing system. The Black Codes created the crimes, the courts convicted, and the county leased the convicted persons to private interests for labor.

The Clarke County courthouse — the same institution whose successor exercises jurisdiction today — processed those convictions. The continuity is not metaphorical. It is institutional.

**EVIDENCE:** Georgia Department of Archives and History, Convict Lease Records — documenting the systematic use of criminal conviction to supply labor to railroad companies, mines, turpentine camps, and farms. The disproportionate conviction of Black men for offenses rarely prosecuted when committed by white men is documented in the conviction records themselves.

**EVIDENCE:** Douglas Blackmon, *Slavery by Another Name* (2008) — Black men convicted of vagrancy, petty theft, and other minor offenses under selectively enforced Black Codes were leased to private interests without compensation. The constitutional prohibition on involuntary servitude was bypassed through the punishment exception.

**PRESENT TENSE:** The Clarke County jail today holds a disproportionate number of Black men. The Stanford Open Policing Project documents racially disparate traffic enforcement in Athens. The Western Judicial Circuit produces sentencing outcomes that reflect the documented national pattern of racial disparity.

**FINDING:** The convict leasing system operated in Clarke County using the county's own legal institutions. Those institutions' successors continue to exercise jurisdiction over the same population's descendants with documented disparate outcomes. The punishment exception to the Thirteenth Amendment that enabled convict leasing has never been repealed.

## PART FOUR: THE REDLINING OF ATHENS — The Economic Architecture of Exclusion

Redlining — the systematic denial of federally backed mortgage guarantees to residents of neighborhoods designated as high risk based on racial composition — was implemented in Athens as it was implemented in cities across the United States.

**EVIDENCE:** Home Owners' Loan Corporation Security Maps, Athens Georgia — HOLC maps designated Black residential neighborhoods as Category D — Hazardous — excluding those areas from federally backed mortgage guarantees. These maps determined who could access the FHA and VA mortgage programs that built the white middle class.

**EVIDENCE:** Ira Katznelson, *When Affirmative Action Was White* (2005) — Documents the systematic exclusion of Black veterans from GI Bill benefits through locally administered programs. The University of Georgia did not admit Black students. The banks redlined Black neighborhoods. The wealth transferred to white veterans was denied to Black veterans.

**PRESENT TENSE:** The neighborhoods that were redlined in Athens remain among the lowest-wealth areas of Clarke County today. The racial wealth gap in Athens-Clarke County reflects the direct compound interest of the redlining decisions made in the 1930s through the 1960s.

**FINDING:** The redlining of Athens systematically excluded Black residents from the primary wealth-building mechanism of the mid-twentieth century. This is not history. This is compound interest on a specific theft.

## PART FIVE: THE 1961 INTEGRATION — Federal Force Required to Honor the Constitution

Brown v. Board of Education had been decided in 1954 — seven years before integration. The Fourteenth Amendment had been ratified in 1868 — ninety-three years before. The University of Georgia required a specific federal court order to do what the Constitution had required for ninety-three years.

On January 11, 1961, white students rioted. They threw rocks and bottles at Hunter's dormitory window. They burned crosses. University officials suspended Hunter and Holmes — the Black students targeted by the riot — on the grounds that their presence was disruptive. The perpetrators were not suspended. The victims were.

**EVIDENCE:** *Holmes v. Danner* — Judge Bootle's order requiring admission, then his order requiring reinstatement. The federal judiciary was required to intervene twice in five days.

**EVIDENCE:** Charlayne Hunter-Gault, *In My Place* (1992) — documents the institutional hostility, the riot, the suspension, the federal reinstatement.

**FINDING:** The 1961 integration required federal judicial intervention because the University would not voluntarily honor a constitutional guarantee that had existed for ninety-three years. The institution's response — suspending the victims rather than prosecuting the rioters — demonstrates that formal compliance did not reflect genuine acceptance of the constitutional principle.

Ninety-three years. A federal court order. A riot. The victims suspended. The rioters protected. A commemoration. A building named. The gaps persist. The chain is lighter.

## PART SIX: THE PRESENT TENSE — Ongoing Enforcement in Athens-Clarke County

The Stanford Open Policing Project found racially disparate patterns in who is stopped, who is searched, and how encounters are resolved. The veil of darkness test — comparing stop rates during daylight hours when race is visible to stop rates at night when it is less visible — shows that racial disparities decrease when the officer cannot easily determine the driver's race. This is evidence of race-based rather than conduct-based enforcement.

Athens-Clarke County has one of the highest poverty rates among Georgia counties. The poverty rate for Black residents substantially exceeds the poverty rate for white residents. The racial disparities in poverty and income reflect the compound effect of the redlining, the convict leasing, the educational exclusion, and the systematic denial of wealth-building opportunity.

The University's tax-exempt status means the county's largest property holder pays no property taxes. The county's Black residents — many of whom live in lower-wealth neighborhoods that were redlined — pay property taxes on properties systematically undervalued by discriminatory appraisal practices.

**FINDING:** The present-tense conditions in Athens-Clarke County are the documented, traceable, compound result of the specific crimes documented in this Indictment. They are not coincidental disparities without cause. They are the ongoing operation of an enterprise whose foundational crimes were never remedied.

## PART SEVEN: THE LIGHTER CHAIN — The Apology That Changes Nothing

The University of Georgia has issued statements acknowledging its history. Clarke County officials have participated in commemorations. The federal government has apologized for redlining.

None of these acknowledgments have been accompanied by restitution proportionate to the benefit received or the harm caused. None of them have changed who holds the land, who controls the institution, who benefits from the endowment, or who experiences the racially disparate outcomes of the legal system.

An apology without remedy is not an apology. It is a management tool. It releases social pressure without releasing the grip. It acknowledges the wrong without bearing its cost.

**FINDING:** The apologies issued have not been accompanied by remedial measures proportionate to the harm caused or the benefit retained. They function as management tools that release social pressure without requiring institutional change. They are the lighter chain.

The apology acknowledges the crime. The remedy would correct it. The apology without remedy is the crime continuing under a different name.

## PART EIGHT: THE LEGAL CLAIMS — What the Law Requires Now

**Claim One:** Unjust Enrichment Against the University of Georgia — The University retains land obtained through the Creek dispossession, infrastructure built by enslaved labor, and an institutional position built through 176 years of racially discriminatory exclusion. The benefit is specific, documented, and quantifiable.

**Claim Two:** Ongoing Badges and Incidents of Slavery — Under *Jones v. Alfred H. Mayer Co.*, 392 U.S. 409 (1968), the Thirteenth Amendment empowers Congress to identify and eliminate all badges and incidents of slavery. 42 U.S.C. §§ 1981, 1982, and 1983 provide the statutory vehicles.

**Claim Three:** Fair Housing Act Violations — The documented history of redlining, combined with the present-tense racial wealth gap as its compound result, supports claims under the Fair Housing Act's disparate impact theory. *Texas Dept. of Housing v. Inclusive Communities Project*, 576 U.S. 519 (2015).

**Claim Four:** Equal Protection — Racially Disparate Enforcement — The documented institutional history of Clarke County's legal system — from the Black Codes through the convict leasing era through the resistance to integration — provides the historical evidence of discriminatory purpose that the *Washington v. Davis* standard requires.

**Claim Five:** International Human Rights — IACHR Petition — The documented pattern constitutes human rights violations cognizable before the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights.

**Claim Six:** RFRA — Spiritual Land Relationship — The Muscogee Creek's spiritual relationship to the land is a sincerely held religious belief protected under RFRA. The continued occupation of ancestral sacred land without consent or restitution constitutes a substantial burden on that sincerely held religious belief.
