### SOVEREIGN (Jurisdictional Restatement) (noun / adjective)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

Having supreme rank, power, or authority. Self-governing and independent. In American constitutional law, sovereignty resides in the people collectively and is exercised through representative government. The federal and state governments are sovereign within their respective spheres. (Black's Law Dictionary, 11th ed.)

**EMBEDDED ASSUMPTION**

Volume One of the Counter-Canon addressed Sovereign at the foundational level — establishing that the people are the ultimate sovereign from whom governmental authority derives. Volume Ten addresses Sovereign at the jurisdictional ceiling — the level at which the living being's sovereign standing operates as a specific legal claim in any proceeding. The embedded assumption the dominant definition carries at this level is that sovereignty is a collective attribute of the people as a whole that individual persons can invoke only through the political process. This assumption prevents the individual living being from asserting their sovereign standing directly in legal proceedings — requiring them instead to work through the representative institutions that the enterprise controls.

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

Sovereign, at the jurisdictional level, describes the living being's inherent authority over their own existence — the self-governing nature of a natural person whose rights precede and are independent of any State's grant. This is not the same as the discredited sovereign citizen claim that individual persons can opt out of all law. It is the constitutionally grounded claim that the living being appears in any legal proceeding not as a subject of the State's authority but as the source of the State's authority — as the sovereign from whom the government's just powers are derived. Declaration of Independence, 1776. Chisholm v. Georgia, 2 U.S. 419 (1793): at the Revolution, the sovereignty devolved on the people, and they are truly the sovereigns of the country. Yick Wo v. Hopkins, 118 U.S. 356 (1886): sovereignty itself is ultimately in the people.

**Authority:** Chisholm v. Georgia, 2 U.S. 419 (1793). Yick Wo v. Hopkins, 118 U.S. 356 (1886). Declaration of Independence (1776). U.S. Const. amend. X. Faretta v. California, 422 U.S. 806 (1975).

**Practical Application:** In every proceeding, state the living being's sovereign standing clearly and on the record. The Declaration of Standing establishes it. The Two Hands Doctrine grounds it. Volume Ten names it at the jurisdictional ceiling.

*The sovereign does not petition the State for rights. The sovereign holds rights that the State was created to protect. When the living being appears in any forum in their own name, grounded in the Creator's prior claim, they appear as the sovereign they have always been.*

### STANDING (noun)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

A party's right to make a legal claim or seek judicial enforcement of a duty or right. To have standing in federal court, a plaintiff must show: injury in fact — an actual or imminent, concrete and particularized injury; causation — a causal connection between the injury and the challenged conduct; and redressability — a likelihood that the requested relief will redress the injury. (Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.S. 555 (1992))

**EMBEDDED ASSUMPTION**

The dominant standing doctrine — as developed through Lujan and its progeny — has been used to close the courthouse doors on exactly the kinds of systemic claims the Odyssey-1 system makes. The requirement that the plaintiff show a concrete, particularized injury to themselves individually — rather than a systemic injury to a class of persons — has been used to prevent challenges to structural racism, institutional discrimination, and the ongoing badges and incidents of slavery from reaching federal courts. The system protects itself through the standing doctrine by requiring a level of individuation that systemic harm cannot always provide.

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

Standing is the living being's right to be heard — a right that derives not from the three-part Lujan test but from the Due Process Clause's guarantee of a meaningful opportunity to be heard before being deprived of any protected interest. Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319 (1976). The Lujan test is a judicially created doctrine — not a constitutional text — that the Supreme Court developed to limit access to federal courts. Massachusetts v. EPA, 549 U.S. 497 (2007), recognized that states have standing to challenge systemic harms even where individual particularization is difficult. And in international forums — the IACHR, the UN Human Rights Committee — the standing requirements are substantially broader. The domestic standing doctrine is the enterprise's gate. The international forums do not use the same gate.

**Authority:** Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.S. 555 (1992). Massachusetts v. EPA, 549 U.S. 497 (2007). Havens Realty Corp. v. Coleman, 455 U.S. 363 (1982). Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319 (1976). IACHR Rules of Procedure.

**Practical Application:** When a domestic court challenges standing, assert the full scope of injury: the specific individual harm AND the systemic harm of which it is a documented instance. When standing is denied domestically, proceed immediately to international forums where the Lujan gate does not apply.

*The right to be heard is not a gift the court grants. It is a right the Creator endowed and the Constitution acknowledged. The international forums are not bound by the domestic standing doctrine.*

### STATUS (noun)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

The legal standing or position of a person, especially as it affects their rights and duties. **Authority:** Declaration of Independence (1776). U.S. Const. amend. XIV. Plyler v. Doe, 457 U.S. 202 (1982). ICCPR, Article 16. Rickey Allan Howard, Declaration of Standing (2026).

### SURETY (noun)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

A person who is primarily liable for the payment of another's debt or performance of another's obligation. A surety is bound with the principal debtor and may be called upon to fulfill the obligation if the principal defaults. Distinguished from a guarantor in that the surety's liability is immediate. (Black's Law Dictionary, 11th ed.)

**EMBEDDED ASSUMPTION**

The dominant definition of surety presents it as a voluntary commercial arrangement. What it does not address is the way the enterprise uses surety relationships to bind the living being to obligations they never voluntarily assumed — through the all-caps name on legal documents and administrative classifications that presume obligations without disclosure.

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

A legitimate surety relationship requires the voluntary, informed assumption of liability — a conscious decision made with full knowledge of the obligation being assumed and the consequences of default. Any surety relationship imposed on the living being without their informed consent, without full disclosure of its terms, and without genuine voluntary agreement fails the foundational requirements of contract law. The Declaration of Standing is the living being's formal repudiation of any surety relationship imposed without their voluntary and informed agreement.

**Authority:** Restatement (Third) of Suretyship and Guaranty. Williams v. Walker-Thomas Furniture Co., 350 F.2d 445 (D.C. Cir. 1965). U.S. Const. amend. XIII. RFRA, 42 U.S.C. § 2000bb.

**Practical Application:** When any proceeding attempts to hold the living being liable as surety for an obligation they did not voluntarily and informedly assume, challenge the voluntary consent element. Assert the Declaration of Standing as the formal repudiation of any undisclosed surety relationship.

*You are surety for what you chose to guarantee. You are not surety for what was imposed on you without your knowledge. Disclosure is the prerequisite of consent. Consent is the prerequisite of obligation.*

### TRUST (noun)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

A fiduciary relationship in which one party holds legal title to property for the benefit of another. The trustee holds legal title and manages the property. The beneficiary holds equitable title and is entitled to the benefit of the property. Trusts may be express — created by the explicit agreement of the parties — or implied by operation of law. (Black's Law Dictionary, 11th ed.)

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

A trust operates over the living being's relationship to public resources and governmental authority on multiple levels. The public trust doctrine — Illinois Central Railroad v. Illinois, 146 U.S. 387 (1892) — establishes that certain resources are held by the government as trustee for the public as beneficiary. As beneficiaries of the public trust, the living being has standing to demand that the trustee administer the trust resources in the beneficiary's interest, not in the interest of private commercial actors who have captured the trustee. A trustee who administers trust property for their own benefit rather than the beneficiary's has breached their fiduciary duty.

**Authority:** Illinois Central Railroad v. Illinois, 146 U.S. 387 (1892). National Audubon Society v. Superior Court, 33 Cal.3d 419 (1983). Restatement (Third) of Trusts. RFRA, 42 U.S.C. § 2000bb.

*The Creator holds you in trust and will give a full accounting. The government holds public resources in trust for you and must give a full accounting. Both trusts are real. Both trustees are accountable. Both accountings are coming.*

A trust imposed by a court on a party who has wrongfully obtained or holds property that in equity and good conscience belongs to another. A constructive trust is not a true trust but a remedy imposed to prevent unjust enrichment. (Black's Law Dictionary, 11th ed.)

Constructive trust doctrine does not require new law. It requires only the application of existing equity principles to documented facts at a scale commensurate with the documented wrong.

**Authority:** Restatement (Third) of Restitution and Unjust Enrichment §§ 55-56. Beatty v. Guggenheim Exploration Co., 225 N.Y. 380 (1919). Jones v. Alfred H. Mayer Co., 392 U.S. 409 (1968).

### UNCONSCIONABLE (adjective)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

Shockingly unfair or unjust. In contract law, a contract or clause is unconscionable when it is so one-sided as to be oppressive and unfair, especially where there is a significant disparity in bargaining power between the parties. An unconscionable contract is unenforceable. The doctrine has both procedural and substantive dimensions. (Williams v. Walker-Thomas Furniture Co., 350 F.2d 445 (D.C. Cir. 1965))

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

Unconscionable describes any arrangement — contractual, statutory, administrative, or systemic — that is so one-sided, so oppressive, and so the product of extreme power imbalance that no honest court can enforce it without betraying the equity principles from which the unconscionability doctrine derives. The social contract imposed on people whose ancestors were excluded from its formation is unconscionable. UCC § 2-302 and the Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 208 provide the doctrinal vehicles. The documented history of the Founding Crime Treatise and the Athens Indictment provide the predicate.

**Authority:** Williams v. Walker-Thomas Furniture Co., 350 F.2d 445 (D.C. Cir. 1965). UCC § 2-302. Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 208. ICCPR, Article 26.

### VENUE (noun)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

The proper or a possible place for a lawsuit to be heard, usually because the place has some connection to the events at issue or the parties. Distinguished from jurisdiction — jurisdiction is the court's power to hear a case; venue is the proper location for that hearing. (Black's Law Dictionary, 11th ed.)

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

Venue is not merely a procedural convenience — it is a determination of which legal culture, which community standards, and which institutional relationships will govern the living being's proceeding. In the broader Odyssey-1 framework, venue extends to the selection among all available forums: domestic or international, federal or state, judicial or administrative, IACHR or UN Human Rights Committee. The Forum Beyond the Forum document is the complete guide to venue selection across all available forums.

**Authority:** 28 U.S.C. §§ 1391-1412. Sheppard v. Maxwell, 384 U.S. 333 (1966). Batson v. Kentucky, 476 U.S. 79 (1986). Rickey Allan Howard, The Forum Beyond the Forum (2026).

### VOID (adjective / noun)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

Having no legal force or effect; null. A void act is an absolute nullity — it is treated as if it never existed and cannot be ratified or given effect by the consent of the parties. Distinguished from voidable, which is an act that may be affirmed or avoided at the election of a party. A void judgment may be attacked collaterally at any time. (Black's Law Dictionary, 11th ed.)

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

Void means having no legal force from the beginning — **void ab initio due to usurpation**. Not challengeable. Not unconstitutional. Not voidable. Void. The Counter-Canon's argument, built across ten volumes, is that the enterprise's most fundamental acts against the living being are void — not merely wrong, not merely correctable through the political process, but void from their inception because they exceeded the authority the enterprise was ever legitimately given. A void judgment may be attacked collaterally at any time — there is no statute of limitations on challenging what is void.

**Authority:** Old Wayne Mut. Life Ass'n v. McDonough, 204 U.S. 8 (1907). Marbury v. Madison, 5 U.S. 137 (1803). United States v. Lee, 106 U.S. 196 (1882). Norton v. Shelby County, 118 U.S. 425 (1886).

**Practical Application:** When the enterprise acts against the living being through an instrument that is void, challenge it as void at any time. There is no statute of limitations on void acts. Norton v. Shelby County is the authority: an unconstitutional act is not a law. It confers no rights. It imposes no duties. It affords no protection to those who acted under it.

*Void means it was never real. Not from the moment of the challenge. From the moment of the act. The accounting that calls them void does not create a new reality. It acknowledges the reality that always existed.*

### VOIDABLE (adjective)

**DOMINANT DEFINITION**

Capable of being affirmed or rejected at the option of one of the parties. A voidable act is not void ab initio but may be avoided or ratified by the party for whose protection the rule rendering it voidable exists. Distinguished from void in that a voidable act has legal effect until avoided. (Black's Law Dictionary, 11th ed.)

**COUNTER-DEFINITION**

The distinction between void and voidable must be drawn by the nature of the defect, not by the institutional interests of the courts drawing it. An act is void when it was outside any legitimate authority from the beginning. An act is voidable when it was within the authority's general power but exercised defectively. The enterprise's acts against the living being of African descent fall in the void category, not the voidable category. They exceeded legitimate authority from the beginning. They were void acts whose continued enforcement does not make them valid but only extends the accounting that is owed.

**Authority:** Restatement (Second) of Contracts §§ 7-8. Old Wayne Mut. Life Ass'n v. McDonough, 204 U.S. 8 (1907). Norton v. Shelby County, 118 U.S. 425 (1886). Marbury v. Madison, 5 U.S. 137 (1803).

*Voidable says: this could have been different if the right party had ratified it. Void says: this was never real from the moment it was done. The enterprise's acts against the living being were never real. They were enforced. Enforced and real are not the same thing.*
